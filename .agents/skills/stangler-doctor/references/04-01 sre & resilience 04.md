Architectural Blueprint for Enterprise Disaster Recovery in Public Cloud Infrastructures Introduction: Resilience and Business Continuity in Cloud Infrastructures In modern cloud computing, system resilience is defined as the capability of an application to maintain correct and consistent execution despite infrastructure, service, or application disruptions, misconfigurations, or transient network anomalies.[1, 2] Disaster recovery is a core component of this resilience strategy, focusing on protecting workloads against less frequent but highly catastrophic failures, such as natural disasters, regional infrastructure outages, and targeted malicious attacks.[1, 2, 3] Under the Shared Responsibility Model for Resiliency, cloud service providers guarantee the integrity and availability of the physical global infrastructure, while the customer is responsible for designing, implementing, and validating the disaster recovery mechanisms required to maintain business continuity.[1] A disaster is formally defined as any event that prevents a workload or system from fulfilling its defined business objectives in its primary deployed location.[3] Mitigating the risks associated with such failures requires organizations to establish a Business Continuity Plan (BCP) that outlines specific recovery strategies.[2] Achieving these objectives in a cloud environment typically necessitates the replication and eventual failover of workloads to an isolated recovery site, such as a different geographical region or an entirely separate cloud account.[1] Foundational Resiliency Metrics: Recovery Objectives and Constraints Designing a disaster recovery architecture requires establishing clear, non-negotiable metrics that define the boundaries of acceptable downtime and data loss.[2, 4] These metrics—specifically the Recovery Time Objective (RTO) and the Recovery Point Objective (RPO)—serve as the engineering constraints that determine the selection of cloud topologies and deployment models.[4] To align these metrics with a comprehensive resiliency strategy, architects must differentiate between high availability and disaster recovery.[2] While high availability focuses on localized, common disruptions (such as component failures or software bugs) and is measured as a mean value over time, disaster recovery addresses catastrophic, one-time events that threaten business continuity.[2] System availability is traditionally modeled using Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF) and Mean Time to Recover (MTTR) [2]: \text{Availability} = \frac{MTBF}{MTBF + MTTR} For request-driven cloud architectures, availability is calculated based on request success rates [2]: \text{Availability} = \frac{\text{Successful Responses}}{\text{Valid Requests}} Conversely, disaster recovery metrics focus strictly on boundary limits following a disaster event.[2] RTO defines the maximum acceptable duration of system downtime before service must be restored in the recovery region.[4, 5] RPO defines the maximum acceptable window of data loss, measured in time, representing the age of the data that must be restored from backups or replicas to resume operations.[4, 5] Continuous data replication across regions is critical for meeting low RPO targets, but replication alone does not protect against data corruption, software bugs, or ransomware attacks.[6, 7] In such events, corrupted data or malicious modifications are immediately replicated to the standby site, rendering the replica unusable.[6, 7] To mitigate this risk, a robust disaster recovery strategy must incorporate Point-in-Time Recovery (PITR) and data versioning.[6, 7] PITR utilizes continuous write-ahead log archiving to enable data restoration to a precise, non-corrupted state, potentially lowering RPO to as low as five minutes in standard backup configurations.[6] Additionally, cross-account backup topologies are essential for cyber resilience.[1] By replicating point-in-time backups to an isolated, separate "clean room" recovery account with distinct credentials, organizations establish a secure repository that remains inaccessible even if the primary production account is fully compromised.[1] Enterprises frequently leverage specialized services like Rubrik to complement native cloud replication, adding immutable data backups, continuous threat monitoring, and automated clean recovery validation to ensure that compromised or encrypted data is not restored during failover.[4] Cloud Disaster Recovery Strategies: Topologies, Regions, and Costs Disaster recovery strategies in the cloud are broadly categorized into active-passive and active-active topologies.[8] Active-passive strategies utilize an active primary region to serve live traffic, while a passive secondary region is maintained as a recovery site that remains dormant until a failover event is triggered.[8] These strategies scale in cost, operational complexity, and recovery objectives.[4, 8] Backup and Restore The backup-and-restore strategy represents the simplest and most cost-effective approach to disaster recovery.[4, 8, 9] Under normal operating conditions, no compute resources are provisioned in the recovery region.[6, 10] Instead, system and database snapshots are generated periodically and replicated asynchronously to storage or archival services in the secondary region.[4, 6, 10] When a disaster is declared, engineers must provision the network topology, deploy virtual servers or containerized environments, deploy application code, and restore the databases from the replicated snapshots.[6] A key operational constraint is that native cloud backup services often lack automated restoration capabilities to secondary regions.[8] Consequently, organizations must implement automated restore scripts using cloud SDKs and APIs to execute snapshot restoration upon failover, ensuring that recovery times do not extend from hours into days.[5, 8] Pilot Light The pilot light strategy minimizes baseline operating costs while achieving a significantly lower RTO than the backup-and-restore model.[6, 8] In this topology, core data infrastructure components—such as database replicas and storage services—are kept "always on" and actively replicating data asynchronously in the recovery region.[6, 8] However, compute resources, application servers, and load balancers remain undeployed or scaled to zero.[6, 10] During a failover, the compute tier is dynamically provisioned and scaled up via Infrastructure as Code (IaC) templates, and routing layers are updated to connect to the active database replica.[5, 6, 10] A critical risk in pilot light architectures is configuration drift.[5] If the standby environment configurations are not continuously updated, virtual machine images or container configurations may become obsolete, causing failover operations to fail.[5] This risk must be mitigated by automating the generation of application images across all regions.[5] Additionally, pilot light strategies carry the risk of compute capacity constraints in the secondary region during a widespread regional outage.[11] To resolve this, organizations can implement a "pilot light with reserved capacity" pattern.[11] By utilizing On-Demand Capacity Reservations in the secondary region, organizations can guarantee instance availability during failover without long-term contracts, optimizing costs by combining reservations with savings plans.[11] Furthermore, cloud-native tools like AWS Elastic Disaster Recovery (AWS DRS) leverage this pilot light approach by utilizing continuous block-level data replication to keep active storage costs low while dynamically orchestrating compute resources during failover drills or actual disasters, achieving RTOs in minutes and RPOs in seconds.[6] Warm Standby The warm standby strategy involves maintaining a fully functional but scaled-down duplicate of the production environment running continuously in the secondary region.[4, 5, 6] Database replication is live, and a minimal fleet of application servers and load balancers are kept online to process background tasks or synthetic test traffic.[5, 6] When a failover occurs, the compute tier is scaled horizontally using auto-scaling policies to handle the full production load.[5, 6] When scaled to full capacity during normal operations, this is referred to as a hot standby.[6] To guarantee that compute capacity is immediately available during scaling, architects must implement capacity reservations or provisioned concurrency configurations.[6] A major operational benefit of warm standby is the ability to conduct continuous testing.[5] Unlike passive strategies, the live endpoints in a warm standby allow automated health checks and synthetic user transactions to run at regular intervals.[5] This continuous validation ensures that any integration failures, data synchronization issues, or configuration errors are detected and resolved immediately during normal operations, rather than during a disaster recovery emergency.[5] Multi-Site Active-Active The multi-site active-active strategy represents the most resilient cloud topology, achieving near-zero RTO and RPO.[4, 6] In this model, fully operational production environments run concurrently in multiple cloud regions, with global traffic directors (such as Route 53) distributing user traffic across all active locations.[4, 12] If one region becomes impaired, global routing policies automatically redirect traffic to the remaining healthy regions without failover delay.[4, 12] However, this model introduces extreme engineering complexity and cost.[6, 7, 11] The geographical distance between regions introduces network propagation latency, making real-time synchronous replication impractical.[7, 13] As a result, active-active systems must rely on asynchronous replication, which introduces replication lag and the risk of write-write conflicts across regions.[7, 12, 13] Operating a multi-site active-active system requires rewriting application logic to support intelligent routing, session affinity, idempotent transactions, and complex conflict resolution algorithms.[7, 12] Multi-Region Database Topologies and Write Strategies When deploying multi-region architectures, managing data consistency across geographical distances is a primary challenge.[7] Multi-region elasticity allows systems to dynamically scale compute, storage, and networking resources across distinct regions, improving latency by placing workloads closer to users.[13] However, this elasticity complicates data synchronization, requiring architects to choose an appropriate database write strategy.[7, 13] Under a write-global strategy, all transactional write operations are routed to a single, designated primary region, while read operations are distributed across read replicas in secondary regions.[7, 8, 13] If the primary region fails, a secondary region must be promoted to accept writes.[7, 8] Aurora Global Database is designed for this pattern, supporting asynchronous replication of committed data to up to five secondary regions with promotion times of less than one minute.[7, 8, 12] Aurora also supports write forwarding, allowing secondary clusters to receive write commands and automatically forward them to the primary write cluster, which simplifies application design.[8] The write-local strategy allows application instances in any region to accept write operations locally, which are then asynchronously replicated to all other regions.[7, 8, 12] Amazon DynamoDB Global Tables represent a write-local implementation, maintaining multi-active replica tables across regions.[7, 8, 12] This approach provides low write latency but relies on eventual consistency, making write-conflict resolution a critical design requirement.[7, 12, 13] The write-partitioned strategy assigns write operations to specific regions based on a partition key (such as user ID or geocoding) to avoid write conflicts.[7, 8] For example, bi-directional S3 replication can be configured between two active regions to support this pattern.[8] When implementing this, architects must enable replica modification sync on the buckets to synchronize metadata changes (such as ACLs, object tags, or object locks) and configure delete marker replication to align with the application's deletion logic.[8] This strategy must also be paired with point-in-time backups to protect against data corruption.[8] Other options for multi-region active-active datastores include Amazon Keyspaces (for Apache Cassandra), which support multi-region replication with low-latency reads and writes, and Amazon ElastiCache global datastores, which enable replication of cache states across AWS regions.[7] For write-intensive workloads, if an active-active model is too complex, architects can utilize a primary-standby database topology or implement a sharding strategy to partition the database, limiting the blast radius of any regional impairment to a subset of the client base.[7] Distributed Systems Theory: CAP and PACELC Theorems Designing multi-region database architectures requires adhering to the fundamental laws of distributed systems, formulated by the CAP and PACELC theorems.[13, 14, 15] The CAP theorem states that a distributed system can simultaneously guarantee only two of three properties: Consistency (C, where every read receives the most recent write or an error), Availability (A, where every non-failing node returns a non-error response), and Partition Tolerance (P, where the system continues to operate despite network splits).[14, 16, 17] Because physical network partitions between geographically distant cloud regions are inevitable, partition tolerance (P) must be assumed.[7, 13, 16] Thus, during a partition, architects must choose between consistency (C) and availability (A).[7, 13, 16] While CAP models system behavior during rare network partitions, the PACELC theorem extends this analysis to normal operating conditions.[14, 15, 18] PACELC states: if there is a Partition (P), how does the system trade off between Availability (A) and Consistency (C); Else (E), when the system is running normally, how does it trade off between Latency (L) and Consistency (C)?[14, 15, 17] These classifications highlight the physical trade-offs of replication latency.[7, 13] Selecting a PC/EC system (such as Google Spanner) ensures strict data consistency across all regions, but requires a full network round-trip for synchronous write replication, which increases write latency by an order of magnitude.[7, 13] Conversely, selecting a PA/EL system (such as ScyllaDB or DynamoDB Global Tables) optimizes latency by performing asynchronous replication.[7, 12, 13] While this ensures sub-millisecond local write times, it introduces replication lag, meaning secondary regions may serve stale data until synchronization completes.[7, 13] For instance, Amazon S3 Replication Time Control (RTC) guarantees replication of 99.99% of objects within 15 minutes, though most replicate within seconds.[13] Applications built on PA/EL databases must handle replication lag at the application layer to maintain read-after-write consistency, ensuring that a user routed to a different region immediately after a write does not encounter stale data.[12] Additionally, data localization is often shaped by regional regulations, such as GDPR.[13] While GDPR does not explicitly mandate data localization, it restricts cross-border data transfers, encouraging organizations to store and process data within the European Economic Area (EEA) to simplify compliance.[13] Multi-Region Write Conflict Resolution: LWW versus CRDTs In active-active multi-region databases where writes are accepted concurrently across geographically separate locations, asynchronous replication is necessary to maintain low write latencies.[13, 20] However, this introduces the risk of write-write conflicts when the same record is modified in multiple regions before replication can propagate the changes.[12, 20, 21] Resolving these conflicts requires robust algorithmic strategies.[12, 20] The simplest and most common strategy is Last Writer Wins (LWW).[12, 20] LWW utilizes physical timestamps to resolve conflicts; when concurrent writes occur, the database retains the write with the latest timestamp and discards the older write.[20] While LWW is computationally simple, it can result in silent, invisible data loss.[20] The application assumes both writes succeeded, but one write is discarded, potentially masking critical business logic conflicts.[20] To avoid this data loss, modern active-active databases implement Conflict-free Replicated Data Types (CRDTs).[20, 21] A CRDT is a data structure with mathematically defined merge rules embedded directly within its type definition.[20] This allows replicas to receive updates independently and concurrently without global coordination.[20, 21] Once replicas exchange states asynchronously, they automatically converge to a consistent state, preserving the semantic intent of all operations.[20, 21] For example, concurrent additions to a set converge into a union of all additions, while counters sum all increments and decrements.[20] In highly performant active-active databases like Amazon MemoryDB Multi-Region, CRDTs are utilized to achieve 99.999% availability with microsecond read and single-digit millisecond write latencies.[21] MemoryDB Multi-Region implements two levels of LWW to resolve conflicts [21]: Key-level LWW: Used for String data types, where the latest executed operation determines the final value of the key.[21] Element-level LWW: Used for collection types (such as Sets, Hashes, or Sorted Sets), where conflicts are resolved on an element-by-element basis.[21] Concurrently adding different elements to the same collection from multiple regions results in the collection containing all elements.[21] In conflict scenarios, if a key is concurrently created with different data types, the operation with the latest timestamp wins.[21] For concurrent create-deletion events, the end result is determined by the timestamp order of the deletion.[21] If a deletion occurs before a new member is added in another region, the set converges to contain only the newly added member.[21] If the deletion occurs after, the key is successfully deleted.[21] The difference between LWW and CRDTs is illustrated by the "Inventory Counter Problem".[20] Suppose an e-commerce warehouse has one (1) unit of an item left in stock, and customers in Tokyo and Frankfurt purchase it at the exact same physical moment, routing their requests to local active-active database replicas.[20] Under the LWW model, both Tokyo and Frankfurt process the orders locally and write an updated inventory value of 0 to their respective replicas.[20] When the regions replicate asynchronously, they both overwrite the key with 0.[20] Because the final state is a consistent 0, the database does not detect a conflict.[20] Both orders are accepted, resulting in an invisible oversell that is only discovered when a customer complains.[20] Under the CRDT model, rather than overwriting the inventory with a static value, both regions record their specific decrement operations (-1 from Tokyo and -1 from Frankfurt).[20] When the states are exchanged, the CRDT sum rule is applied: 1 + (-1) + (-1) = -1 The inventory converges to -1.[20] This negative value acts as an explicit signal in the database, allowing the application to immediately detect the conflict, flag the double-sale, and initiate a refund or backorder.[20] Infrastructure as Code: Terraform Best Practices for Multi-Region DR Managing a multi-region disaster recovery architecture requires Infrastructure as Code (IaC) to maintain consistency and eliminate configuration drift.[5, 9, 10] Terraform is widely adopted for this purpose, utilizing HCL declarations to define multi-region environments.[10, 22, 23] To manage infrastructure across multiple regions within a single Terraform configuration, developers must utilize provider aliases.[22, 23] A default provider is defined for the primary region, while additional provider blocks use the alias argument to target secondary regions.[22, 23] Because Terraform providers are static and cannot be dynamically generated using resource loops like for_each, architects must explicitly declare every regional provider block in the root configuration.[22, 23] To organize these, architects can map regions to static provider blocks using a local map variable.[23] When utilizing modules to deploy identical application stacks across regions, the root module must explicitly pass the aliased providers to the child modules via the providers map block [22, 23]: Within child modules, developers must declare the required provider specifications within the terraform block to ensure compatibility.[23] If a child module uses provider aliases internally, the root module must pass all required providers to avoid configuration errors.[23] A critical decision when designing multi-region IaC pipelines is the structure of the Terraform state files.[23] Architects must choose between a single, centralized state file or separate, region-specific state files.[23] During active-passive cutovers, the -refresh-only flag is highly useful, allowing developers to update the local Terraform state to match the live infrastructure without modifying the resources themselves, reducing drift after failover.[9] To secure the state storage, the backend S3 bucket must have versioning and cross-region replication enabled to remain accessible if the primary region experiences an outage.[10, 24] Additionally, continuous drift detection (using platforms like Spacelift paired with Open Policy Agent) must be integrated into the CI/CD pipeline to automatically flag manual infrastructure modifications or state inconsistencies.[10] Architects must also account for global cloud services, such as IAM, Route 53, and CloudFront.[23] These services are global and should only be provisioned once in the configuration to prevent duplication.[23] However, some services have region-specific requirements; for example, ACM certificates used for CloudFront distributions must always be created in the us-east-1 region, regardless of where the main application resources reside.[23] Automated Failover: Scripting, Health Checks, and Routing Controls Executing a multi-region failover during an outage requires a clear architectural division between the control plane and the data plane of cloud routing services.[25] The control plane is the administrative layer used to create, modify, or delete configuration resources, such as updating a DNS record or changing a health check configuration.[25] Regional cloud services have regional control planes, but global services (like Route 53, CloudFront, and IAM) centralize their control planes in a single primary region (e.g., us-east-1 for Route 53, or us-west-2 for Route 53 Application Recovery Controller).[25] During a major regional outage, these centralized control planes may become degraded or inaccessible.[25] Conversely, the data plane is the globally distributed runtime layer that actively processes client queries, executes health checks, and forwards traffic.[25] The Route 53 data plane is designed for 100% availability SLAs.[25] Consequently, a primary best practice for disaster recovery is that failover mechanisms must rely entirely on data plane functions.[25] If a failover design requires modifying DNS records or updating health check configurations during an outage, it introduces a dependency on a centralized control plane, which is highly likely to fail when the provider is experiencing widespread issues.[25] Instead, failover must be triggered by interacting with pre-configured data plane switches, such as Route 53 health check states or Route 53 Application Recovery Controller (ARC) routing controls, which operate entirely within the highly resilient data plane.[25] Additionally, failover operations must be controlled entirely from the healthy standby region to avoid any dependencies on the impaired primary region.[25] Organizations should pre-provision critical components to handle full or partial loads in advance, avoiding control plane dependencies during an event.[25] Authentication methods must also be reviewed; for example, if regional Single Sign-On (SSO) is impaired, engineers can maintain access using pre-provisioned entitlements in other regions or by pre-configuring a "break glass" IAM user, as IAM authentication relies on the globally available IAM data plane.[25] To ensure high reliability, automated DNS failover relies on Route 53 health checks.[25] Route 53 health checkers poll endpoints from eight geographically diverse regions.[25] To protect against false positives caused by temporary local network issues (known as route flapping), Route 53 utilizes a quorum validation threshold.[25] By default, at least 3 out of 16 global health checkers (representing 18.75% of the probing fleet) must report the endpoint as healthy for Route 53 to consider the resource online.[25] If the primary endpoint fails this quorum check, Route 53 automatically stops returning the primary DNS record and fails over to the pre-provisioned standby record.[25] These health checks can monitor TCP/HTTP/HTTPS endpoints, other health checks, CloudWatch alarms, or Route 53 ARC routing controls.[25] For private contexts, organizations can reduce external internet dependencies by using CloudWatch metric alarms over a VPC endpoint in a private hosted zone, initiating failovers completely within the private network.[25] While automated DNS failover works well for active-active topologies, active-passive failover requires more control to prevent split-brain scenarios and bad deployments.[25, 26] For this reason, enterprises utilize Route 53 Application Recovery Controller (ARC).[27] ARC has three main components [27]: Readiness Checks: ARC continuously evaluates the resource configurations, capacities, and limits of the standby region against the primary region.[27, 28] If a secondary region has an Auto Scaling group with a maximum capacity of 5 instances while the primary is configured for 20, or if a target group is missing its health check configuration, the readiness check flags the resource as NOT_READY, alerting engineers to capacity mismatches before a disaster occurs.[27] Routing Controls: Routing controls act as highly available data plane switches linked directly to Route 53 health checks.[27] When an operator or script sets a routing control state to ON via a simple API call to a highly available five-region data plane cluster, the associated health check reports as healthy, and traffic flows to that region.[27, 29] Setting it to OFF forces the health check to report as unhealthy, shifting DNS traffic away.[26, 27] Changes are coordinated across cluster endpoints, requiring at least three of the five endpoints to be available to establish a quorum.[25, 29] Safety Rules: Safety rules act as programmatic guardrails to prevent human error during highly stressful outage scenarios.[27] For example, an assertion rule can enforce that "at least one region's routing control must be ON at all times".[27] If an engineer attempts to set both routing controls to OFF simultaneously, the ARC API rejects the command, preventing a complete blackout of the application.[27] To orchestrate the complex sequence of actions required during a regional failover, organizations utilize AWS Step Functions and Lambda.[29] When a failover is initiated, these state machines execute commands in a precise, deterministic order: first, they disable routing controls in the primary region to stop write operations [29]; next, they executedatabase promotion API calls in the standby region [29]; and finally, they enable the standby region's routing controls to begin routing client traffic.[29] This orchestrated workflow ensures that data integrity is maintained, and split-brain scenarios are avoided during a failover event.[29]Additionally, organizations can use ARC zonal shifts to isolate and recover from single Availability Zone (AZ) impairments.[28] Zonal shift temporarily redirects traffic away from an impaired AZ to healthy AZs in the same region, mitigating the impact of local failures or bad deployments.[28] ARC zonal autoshift automates this process, authorizing AWS to shift traffic away from an impaired AZ when internal telemetry indicates a localized issue.[28]At the application level, failover orchestration must also be integrated into the CI/CD pipeline.[26] In an active/standby setup, CodePipeline, CodeBuild, and CodeDeploy can be configured to deploy application changes to one region at a time.[26] By implementing a manual approval step before propagating changes to the standby region, organizations can verify successful deployment in the active region first, preventing a bad version from propagating to both regions and avoiding correlated failures.[26]Conclusion: Strategic Architectural GuidanceBuilding an enterprise-grade disaster recovery architecture requires balancing business continuity objectives with technical constraints and implementation costs.[2, 6] Architects must treat disaster recovery as an integrated component of business continuity planning, aligning technical designs with the organizational RTO and RPO requirements.[2]To build a resilient multi-region infrastructure, organizations must adhere to several strategic principles:Decouple DR from Control Planes: Failover orchestration and traffic redirection mechanisms must rely entirely on distributed data plane functions, such as Route 53 ARC routing controls and pre-provisioned data plane switches, ensuring high availability during regional outages.[25]Mitigate Configuration Drift: For Pilot Light and Warm Standby topologies, organizations must automate the continuous building and replication of application images across regions, ensuring that standby environments are always ready to accept production loads.[5]Incorporate Cyber Resilience: Point-in-Time Recovery (PITR) and data versioning must be implemented alongside continuous replication to protect against ransomware and data corruption, with backups stored in an isolated, separate "clean room" cloud account.[1, 6]Conduct Regular testing: Workloads must be designed and reviewed against the AWS Well-Architected Framework, specifically Reliability Pillar question REL 13.[2] Disaster recovery plans should be tested quarterly using automated failover drills and continuous synthetic testing to verify system readiness.[5, 10]

Disaster Recovery (DR) is an essential part of cloud architecture. It ensures that your workloads remain available even when an entire AWS region experiences downtime. In this post, I'll walk you through how to design and deploy a multi-region disaster recovery setup using Terraform while following AWS best practices. Why Multi-Region DR Matters Relying on a single AWS region can be risky. Hardware failures, outages, or network disruptions in one region can make your application unavailable. A multi-region architecture provides redundancy by replicating critical resources across regions. In this example, we'll use us-east-1 as the primary region and eu-west-1 as the disaster recovery region. Region Switch is a fully managed service for multi-Region recovery orchestration. It includes features to handle DNS failover (topic of this blog post). For many applications, deploying to multiple Availability Zones in a single AWS Region and aligning with the AWS Well-Architected Framework offers the right balance of availability, simplicity, and affordability. However, some customers may have regulatory or other reasons that require expanding to additional Regions in an active-active or active-standby disaster recovery (DR) configuration. In a DR scenario, you need a simple and effective approach to mitigate failures and then return to normal operations. To be fast and reliable, this process must be straightforward, require minimal steps, and be practiced regularly. To failover, many companies change their DNS records. This blog post describes how to use updates to DNS for effective disaster recovery, and highlights principles and best practices you should adopt when following this approach. Modern DNS services, like Amazon Route 53, offer health checks and failover records that you can use to simplify and strengthen your DR plan. We'll start by outlining how AWS services provide reliability using control planes and data planes, then share high-level design principles for creating a failover mechanism. Finally, we'll explain the features of Route 53 that can make your DR approach more effective. Control planes and data planes Most AWS services include a data plane, which provides the service's core functionality, and a control plane, which enables you to create, update, and delete resources. Regional AWS services, such as Amazon Elastic Compute Cloud (Amazon EC2), have Regional service endpoints that connect you to the service's control plane in a specific AWS Region. However, some AWS services are distributed across many locations around the globe, such as Amazon CloudFront, AWS Identity and Access Management (IAM), and Route 53. The control plane for these global services are only available in the US East (N. Virginia) Region (us-east-1). Similarly, AWS Global Accelerator and Amazon Route 53 Application Recovery Controller (Route 53 ARC) are global services with control planes located in the US West (Oregon) Region (us-west-2). To create and configure resources on these global services, you must use the API endpoint in the Region where their control plane is located. The Route 53 data planes answer DNS queries, and perform and evaluate health checks. They are globally distributed and designed for a 100% availability service level agreement (SLA). The Route 53 management APIs and consoles where you create, update, and delete Route 53 resources run on control planes that are designed to prioritize the strong consistency and durability that you need when managing DNS. To achieve this, the control planes are located in a single Region, US East (N. Virginia). While both systems are built to be very reliable, the control planes are not included in the SLA. There could be rare events in which the data plane's resilient design allows it to maintain availability while the control planes do not. For this reason, we recommend disaster recovery and failover mechanisms use data plane functions to provide the best possible reliability. For more information about data planes, control planes, and how AWS builds services to meet high availability targets, see the Static stability using Availability Zones paper and the Amazon Builders' Library. Since the Route 53 has a data plane designed for a 100% availability SLA, good failover mechanisms should rely on the data plane and not the control plane. Later in this post, we'll review ways that you can make targeted changes to Route 53 that don't involve the control plane. But first let's look at some best practices to follow when creating an effective DR failover mechanism. Designing a reliable failover mechanism When you design a disaster recovery plan, it's important to consider how you will make sure that the failover mechanism is resilient and available when you need it. These mechanisms should be simple, have the fewest dependencies possible, and be thoroughly tested. While it may seem obvious, it's easy to overlook dependencies that work during a test, but fall into a failed state during a disaster. To create an analogy from the physical world, you need a backup power generator most when utility power isn't available. Therefore, a reliable failover mechanism must not depend on utility power to start up nor to fail over power to itself. In addition, because utility power is typically available when you run tests with your failover setup, you must be sure that you don't have a hidden dependency on that utility power. Consider the following principles as you build a mechanism for failover. Then in the next sections, we'll look at Route 53-based solutions that incorporate these principles. Use data plane functions. Design your failover mechanisms to use data plane functions for the greatest reliability and fault tolerance. For example, choose mechanisms that use Route 53 health checks (data plane functions) rather than updating DNS records in hosted zones or health check configurations (control plane functions). Control failover from your standby Region. Multi-Region failover is powerful because we build each AWS Region to be independent and isolated from other Regions. When you design mechanisms to fail over from an impaired application in one Region, make sure that you do not need to make changes in the impaired Region. Instead, initiate failover by making changes in the healthy standby Region. Understand and reduce dependencies to make failover more reliable. Map out all internal and external dependencies that you'll require to initiate and coordinate a successful failover. Minimizing the number of dependencies in a failover process reduces potential points of error, making it more reliable. For example, use AWS CLI or SDK calls in your failover process instead of the AWS Management Console, as the console is an added UI component that introduces further dependencies. Pre-provision critical components. Avoid control plane dependencies for creating critical components during an event by pre-provisioning resources to handle full or partial loads. Doing so helps achieve disaster recovery objectives (RTO and RPO), but requires balancing cost against recovery objectives. Whenever possible, creating and scaling resources beforehand also helps prevent capacity constraints in the standby Region. Review authentication methods. Think through how you authenticate for managing your AWS services. For example, an AWS Organization can support AWS Single Sign-On (AWS SSO) in multiple Regions. If AWS SSO is impaired in a Region, then you can continue to have access to AWS accounts using the already provisioned entitlements in the additional Regions, but this requires additional configuration. Another option is pre-configuring a break glass IAM user, as IAM authentication relies on the globally-available IAM data plane. Test regularly. To help instill confidence that your failover mechanism will work when there's a disaster, it's important that you test the process regularly. Testing verifies that your DR plans are up-to-date and can be carried out successfully when needed. Now let's look at three ways that you can use different Route 53 features to follow these principles and create a failover mechanism as part of a disaster recovery plan. Route 53 health checks Route 53 health checks make requests to your resources from eight different AWS Regions to verify their availability. We have integrated health checks into the globally distributed Route 53 data plane for extreme reliability. A Route 53 health check can monitor the following: A TCP, HTTP, or HTTPS endpoint The status of other Route 53 health checks The status of an Amazon CloudWatch alarm The state of a Route 53 Application Recovery Controller routing control Route 53 health checks return as healthy as long as at least 18% of global health checkers report that the endpoint is responding healthy. By default, at least 3 out of 16 checkers (18.75%) must report as healthy for Route 53 to consider the endpoint healthy. These safeguards help to prevent erroneous failures. There must be consistent, repeated failures to turn a health check unhealthy. A health check won't fail if it is flapping between healthy and unhealthy, or if a small subset of the checkers report as unhealthy, for example, because of local network issues. You can set both the health checker Regions and check failure thresholds. You can configure DNS to automatically fail over by using a failover routing policy with health checks. In this scenario, Route 53 failover records return the primary resource for a DNS query when the resource is healthy. If the resource becomes unhealthy and the secondary is healthy, Route 53 automatically updates and responds with the secondary record (that is, it fails over). If both the primary and secondary records are unhealthy, it returns the primary record. Figure 1 shows an active-passive setup with Route 53 DNS failover. An issue in the primary Region resulting in a failed health check (HC-Primary) initiates an automatic failover to the secondary record. Figure 1. Simple automated DNS failover with Route 53 health checks. Automatic recovery through health checks is simple to configure and highly reliable, with no dependencies on the Route 53 control plane. When a health check changes status, Route 53 updates the record associated with the health check using the Route 53 data plane. However, if you use health checks to fail over automatically, you won't be able to force a failover if there are intermittent issues. In addition, you should not depend on the Route 53 heath check APIs to force a failover as that would add a control plane dependency. The direct health checking of resources described here fits best in active-active architectures. In these scenarios, the job of the health checks is simply to remove one failing endpoint from a group of active endpoints. If the endpoint later begins passing health checks, it should be safe to bring it back into service automatically. In this setup, failover and reversion are both automatic and require no user intervention. However, for most disaster recovery plans, an active-standby architecture is more common. A successful failover is often part of a sequence of steps, such as a database failover and scaling up standby capacity. In a multi-step recovery process, you need more direct control over failover and fail back than automated health checking provides. While Route 53 health checks are often used to directly probe the health of resources, you can also use them as a signaling system. By configuring health checks to check other web services that you control, such as the existence of files in S3 buckets, you can reliably signal the Route 53 DNS data plane to initiate failover. The next two sections explain how to take advantage of this so you can control the failover directly. Route 53 Application Recovery Controller Amazon Route 53 Application Recovery Controller (Route 53 ARC) gives you full control of when highly available applications fail over. It provides two features to manage and successfully fail over: readiness checks and routing controls. Readiness checks help make sure your standby systems are ready to fail over to, and routing controls enable you to initiate a failover, manually or programmatically, using a highly available data plane API without any dependency on the Route 53 control plane. A Route 53 ARC routing control is an on/off switch that changes the state of a Route 53 health check, which updates a DNS entry and redirects traffic, for example, from a primary to a standby deployment. We host your routing controls on a dedicated cluster that creates a data plane across five geographically-isolated AWS Regions. The cluster operates on a quorum model, which means that the cluster and routing controls continue to function and provide failover control even if up to two Regional endpoints are unavailable. Each cluster includes five independent API endpoints, one in each Region, and you can update a routing control state by using any of the five endpoints. Route 53 ARC routing controls have the least number of dependencies, and each dependency is extremely reliable on its own. When you make changes to routing control states, you rely on the three criteria below that are highly unlikely to fail: At least three of the five endpoints are available and take part in the quorum. You have working IAM credentials and can authenticate against a working Regional cluster endpoint. The Route 53 data plane is healthy (this data plane is designed to meet a 100% availability SLA). When you update a routing control state, the change propagates through Route 53 health checks and then to your DNS record in Route 53. Since Route 53 ARC's routing controls are designed to be highly available and there are no control plane API operations involved, the change is executed reliably. Routing controls also include configurable safety rules that enable you to define guard rails for failover operations. With safety rules, you can define guard rails explicitly so that the cluster enforces them and refuses any state change that breaks a rule. Some examples are the following: In an active-standby architecture, you might have a rule that only one endpoint (either active or standby) can be enabled and in service at a time. In an active-active architecture, you might have a rule that limits the number of application replicas that are taken offline at a time. This helps prevent different operators or automation components from reducing capacity below safe limits. Changes are processed in the order that they are requested, with checks to make sure that the required number of replicas are still in service. Route 53 ARC readiness checks audit resource quotas, capacity, and other configurations on your application replicas. These checks help keep your application configurations aligned, in case you need to fail over to a replica. For example, in active-passive architectures, you might find that your primary application must be scaled up or otherwise changed. Readiness checks provide a reliable mechanism to make sure that when changes are made to your primary, your standby is also scaled up. Figure 2 illustrates a Route 53 ARC deployment with an application in an active-standby configuration: Figure 2. Route 53 Application Recovery Controller setup with two application replicas You can see that Region A (Primary) and Region B (Standby) have identical applications with an Application Load Balancer, EC2 Auto Scaling group, and a DynamoDB table. In addition, note the following: We've created readiness checks that audit the failover replica in the standby Region on an ongoing basis. We've created EventBridge rules to notify us if individual readiness checks return NOT READY values. If an issue arises with the application in Region A, we can make a routing control API call to any of the five Route 53 ARC cluster data plane endpoints in Region 1-5. When we update the routing control state, that changes the health check status on the Route 53 resource record for our application, and moves traffic to Region B. After you've set up your application with Route 53 ARC routing controls for failover, make sure that you're ready when there's an unexpected event. For example, you should plan to use routing control API operations with the highly available Route 53 ARC data plane to get and update routing control states for production updates. You should also avoid dependencies on control planes, such as the Route 53 ARC console or the recovery control configuration API operations, which are only available in the US West (Oregon) Region (us-west-2). For full guidance about preparing to use Route 53 ARC in a disaster scenario, see Best practices in the Route 53 ARC Developer Guide. Route 53 ARC offers the most feature-rich failover solution of those discussed in this post. However, in some cases it may be more complex to set up, and the multi-Region Route 53 ARC cluster might cost more than other solutions. Let's next look at a simple and cost-effective way of using independent Route 53 health checks to initiate a failover from a standby Region. Standby takes over primary Our third recommendation for failover using Route 53 features is a process we call “standby takes over primary” (STOP). This strategy depends on a healthy standby Region and application. With this solution, you use a resource (a health check) in the standby Region to control the failover. This allows you to initiate a failover without depending on any resources in the primary Region. At a minimum, this solution uses one health check, which checks the status of a resource in the standby Region. Let's look at an example of this architecture. We start with a Route 53 health check, HC-Initiate-Disaster-Recovery, that is associated with the Route 53 DNS record for our application in the primary Region (Region A). This health check looks for a condition that enables an operator (or automation) to declare the primary application “unhealthy” and initiates failover. The condition is something simple; in our case, we use the presence or absence of a file that we create or delete in the standby Region. Specifically, the health check looks for a file on a public S3 website: https://<FAILOVER-BUCKET>.s3.<STANDBY-REGION>.amazonaws.com/initiate-failover.html If the file doesn't exist, the (inverted) health check result is “healthy” and the failover doesn't happen. We use an inverted health check to safeguard against accidentally initiating a failover if the resource in the standby Region fails. The health check is associated with a DNS record in the primary Region (the application), but we configure the inverted health check to probe the health of a resource in the standby Region (the file), which we use to control the health check's state. To initiate a failover, we create the S3 file that the health check looks for: “initiate-failover.html”. This tells the health check that the primary Region is “unhealthy”, causing it to switch traffic from the primary Region (Region A) to the secondary Region (Region B). Figure 3 illustrates the configuration of Route 53 health checks, routing policies, and failover records for this “standby takes over primary” failover strategy: Figure 3. Standby takes over primary failover strategy The health check has the following configuration: Health check: HC-Initiate-Disaster-Recovery Associated with the DNS record of the application in the primary Region. Checks a resource located in the standby Region. Inverted health check is always healthy if the response is a HTTP 4xx, 5xx, or timeout. This makes sure that traffic continues to go toward the primary Region. An operator-controlled initiation activity (such as creation of a file) updates the resource to return HTTP 2xx or 3xx to initiate a failover. To fail over within a private context, one alternative to the S3 file trigger is to use an Amazon CloudWatch metric alarm. With this option, we create an alarm that watches a specific CloudWatch metric in the standby Region, and then create a Route 53 health check that monitors the CloudWatch alarm. As with the S3 option, we use an inverted health check to ensure that the standby Region only takes over when the standby is healthy and posts a specific metric value. Using a CloudWatch alarm as the failover trigger enables us to fail over completely within an Amazon Virtual Private Cloud (VPC). For example, we can call CloudWatch on a VPC endpoint, create DNS entries for health checks in a Route 53 private hosted zone, and then perform failover, in private, within the VPC. We recommend that, if possible, you align your failover initiation activity with existing dependencies for your standby application. For example, if your standby application already depends on read/write access to S3, you won't be adding a new dependency by using an initiation activity that puts a file that you create in an S3 bucket. Alternatively, if your standby application depends on DynamoDB, you might choose to create a health check handler in your application that responds based on a record that you update in a DynamoDB table. You can easily integrate your failover initiation activity into your existing failover automation if, for example, you already use an AWS Systems Manager automation document to prepare resources for failover. Add a new action to the end of the document that invokes the failover initiation activity, and then an operator can run the automation to both prepare the resources and to fail over. When your primary stack has recovered, you have two options to revert. If you want to fail back to the primary Region, you revert the change you made to cause Route 53 to switch back. Alternatively, if you prefer to avoid the impact of a second failover, you can update the Route 53 record sets to swap the failover record set roles of primary and standby. Because this solution uses Route 53 health checks and the failover routing type, it includes the controls and safety checks of those Route 53 features. In addition, using this strategy means minimal costs, because it primarily relies on Route 53 health check pricing. If you need to audit your readiness for failover regularly (which we recommend), Route 53 ARC readiness checks also work with this failover pattern. Summary In this blog post, we described three disaster recovery strategies that you can consider by using Amazon Route 53 features to fail over quickly and reliably, to minimize downtime from an event. We discussed the tradeoffs that come with each strategy. Route 53 health checks are globally distributed and work reliably even during significant service events. Route 53 Application Recovery Controller's data plane is multi-Region and can tolerate the failure of up to two of its Regional endpoints. Finally, the “standby takes over primary” approach ensures that you can fail over without any dependency on your primary Region. Each of these strategies give you options to consider as you create disaster recovery mechanisms that are resilient and limit dependencies for the systems that you rely on during failures.
