Strategy #1: Creating a documentation lifecycle system Documentation strategies should follow a standard lifecycle system. This is to provide structure and clarity regarding the life of every document. With clear context, documentation becomes transparent. This should help provide insights into a document's version and validity. Complying with a standardized documentation lifecycle ensures documents progress in a controlled sequence, maintaining accuracy, reliability, and accountability. Sample documentation lifecycle system Draft: The initial creation of a documentation whose contents are unverified and incomplete In Review: The validation stage of a document, where accuracy and clarity are ensured Published: Approved documentation and an active source of truth for MSPs Stale: Automatically flags published documentation after a set period (e.g., 90 days) for re-verification purposes Archived: Retired documentation replaced by an updated version, but stored as a reference Sorting according to tags and criticality While lifecycle stages ensure documentation remains current, not all documents require the same level of attention. For this reason, MSPs should also tag documentation by criticality. This ensures that vital strategies, particularly for backup and disaster recovery strategies, are reviewed more frequently than general references. Strategy #2: Assigning ownership and editorial oversight Assign a specific owner for clear accountability and transparency regarding documentation tasks. Additionally, role separation streamlines documentation creation by allowing multiple teams to tackle different segments simultaneously. Sample role separation Before an in review draft passes as published, an owner should review and approve changes. Strategy #3: Using automation for freshness checks and metadata updates Technicians can leverage PowerShell scripts to flag documents exceeding a specific amount of time without updates. Schedule scripts to regularly run queries and automate documentation status scans. Sample PowerShell scripts to spot stale documentation: The following script lists any documentation files that have not been modified within the specified threshold (e.g., 90 days). For each file, you will see the word Stale, the full file path, and the last modified date so you can easily understand why it was flagged. Get-ChildItem "C:\Docs\Clients" -Recurse | Where-Object { $_.LastWriteTime -lt (Get-Date).AddDays(-90) } | ForEach-Object { Write-Output "Stale: (.FullName) | Last modified: (.LastWriteTime)" } 💡 Note: Replace C:\Docs\Clients with an existing documentation storage path and change 90 to define the review threshold in days. Strategy #4: Implementing access control and version histories Documentation editing privileges should be limited to owners, editors, and approved contributors, reducing unapproved changes caused by drive-by edits. Clear version naming conventions (e.g., v2025-09-25) are also important when tracking documentation history. Additionally, leveraging transparent version control systems on capable platforms allows for clearer tracking of changes over time. Pair this with color-coded update indicators to help technicians spot stale documentation at a glance. Each dashboard entry should also tie into ownership and review cycles to remind technicians and owners of upcoming review schedules. Start your free trial MSP Executive Playbook FAQs Where do I start if my MSP has no documentation system at all? First, backup and disaster recovery procedures, onboarding checklists, and common troubleshooting steps. Don't try to document everything at once. Pick the five to ten processes your team executes most frequently or that would cause the most damage if done incorrectly, document those first, and build from there. How long does it take to set up a documentation lifecycle? For a small MSP with a modest knowledge base, expect two to four weeks to audit, categorize, and assign ownership to existing docs. For larger teams with years of accumulated documentation, budget four to eight weeks. What's the easiest first step to organize existing docs? Do a simple audit. List every document you currently have, note when it was last updated, and assign a status — current, needs review, or delete. Don't edit anything yet. Just getting visibility into what exists, what's stale, and what's missing gives you a clear starting point and prevents you from building a new system on top of a broken foundation. What tools do MSPs actually use for documentation? The most widely used dedicated documentation platforms are IT Glue and Hudu. IT Glue is the long-standing market leader with deep PSA and RMM integrations and enterprise-grade features. Many MSPs also use NinjaOne's built-in documentation tool, particularly if they're already on the platform, since it keeps documentation, endpoint management, and automation in one place. Who should be responsible for MSP documentation? Each document should have a designated owner (typically a team lead or senior technician) who approves all changes before publication. Contributors draft or suggest updates, editors validate accuracy, and owners give final approval. Without clear ownership, documentation gaps and stale content accumulate unchecked. But as AI agents accelerate the pace of coding, they also amplify the risk of security bottlenecks. At Snyk, we believe a superior Developer Experience (DX) is the only way to secure this new frontier. DX is not just a layer on top of the product. It is the foundation that allows developers to unleash AI innovation securely. We think of DX as a system of decisions that compound over time. Every interaction, every default, and every piece of information a developer encounters shapes how effectively they can use our platform. The five principles that emerged from our journey of evolving and refining the Snyk platform now serve as the foundation for delivering an excellent DX. These principles continuously guide the thousands of small decisions we make across the entire product surface, underscoring our commitment to this ongoing process. Go to where developers work, don't ask them to come to you The most common challenge in developer tooling is the assumption that a beautiful dashboard is the primary destination. Experience shows that developers prioritize their existing workflows, which they have optimised over the years: their IDE, the terminal, their Git flow, and their pull request (PR) process. Context switching out of those workflows has a tremendous cost. We saw this directly at Snyk. We had built a detailed findings interface in the Snyk platform with prioritized vulnerability lists, remediation guidance, and full data flow traces. Developers did not visit it. We learned that even the most valuable data is often bypassed if it requires a context switch. By moving security into the existing PR conversation, we aligned with the developer's natural flow. We changed our model. We stopped asking developers to come to Snyk and started bringing Snyk to them. Security findings became part of the pull request conversation, surfaced directly in the SCM in the same thread where code review was already happening. Same information. Zero context switch, but dramatically different adoption. The principle goes beyond PRs. It is why we invest heavily in IDE plugins, AI coding assistant and, CLI integrations, and CI/CD gates. The question we ask is always the same: where is the developer already working, and how do we show up there? There's a broader shift underway from traditional IDEs to agentic development environments. At the velocity that AI coding assistants drive, context switching becomes a much bigger bottleneck, since higher agent productivity amplifies the cost of breaking flow. As agentic platforms become a core part of developer workflows, Snyk is already integrated in those environments to secure AI-generated code at inception. Developers are not security specialists, so speak their language When we designed security findings in PRs, we optimized for the developer's mental mode. CVSS scores or CWE classifications made sense to security professionals, but to developers they were jargon that required translation. We surface a contextual, natural-language description generated from Snyk's own data flow analysis. For a SQL injection vulnerability, for example, rather than citing a generic advisory, we would explain that unsanitised user input from the HTTP request body is directly interpolated into an SQL query string, naming the source, the sink, and the mechanism in the developer's own code. That one sentence tells a developer, who is often not a security specialist, exactly what and where (to the exact file and line number) the problem is, in terms they already understand. The full trace is still available for those who want it. But most developers do not need to go deeper. They need to understand enough to act. And every surface of Snyk's product attempts to apply this principle. We aim to answer, "What does this developer need to understand, at this moment, given what they know?" Every piece of information is either signal or noise – there's no middle ground There is a tendency in security tools to surface everything. It feels comprehensive, but it often overwhelms rather than helps. When we examined our PR experience, we reframed the problem: what information truly belongs in the developer's view? We chose to be deliberate. What we show depends on the workflow. In prevention, developers need fast, actionable guidance. In remediation, they need depth and more paths when they are optimising for risk reduction. In a PR, every piece of information should either answer an immediate question or enable a clear next step. This context matters a lot as developers in a PR are focused on shipping the functionality. Vulnerability resolution becomes secondary. This is very different from a backlog context, where fixing issues is the primary task. Progressive disclosure also helps balance this. The primary view focuses on the issue, its severity, and the next step. Deeper layers provide additional context, such as data flows, when needed. This keeps the experience focused and noiseless. Detection is not the product, resolution is For a long time, security tools measured success by what they found. The more vulnerabilities surfaced, the more complete the tool felt. What this metric missed was the thing that actually mattered: whether those vulnerabilities got fixed. Most developers do not want awareness. They want to know what to do next. A vulnerability report with no clear next step is just noise with a severity score, and developers, quite rationally, learn to treat it that way. The motivation behind building fix suggestions directly into the PR experience was to close the loop: not just identify vulnerabilities, but fix them without ever leaving the workflow. When Snyk detects a vulnerability in code, it does not just flag it. It proposes a concrete, AI-generated fix as a diff, inline in the PR as a review comment, red lines out, green lines in, applicable as a commit with a single action. For the SQL injection example, rather than flagging the string interpolation and leaving the developer to figure out the solution, the AI Fix suggestion replaces it with a parameterized query. The developer does not need to research secure SQL practices, the fix is already there. The path to resolution becomes the default path. Good DX tells them how to fix an issue, but a great DX makes fixing the default path. Trust is built when developers understand why, not just what When we launched the suggested fix, we saw a pattern repeatedly in developer feedback: the question was not "does the fix work?" It was "Why does this fix work?" Developers were applying suggestions and then struggling to explain them to colleagues. The fix was solving the immediate problem and creating a different one. So we added something that turned out to be one of the highest-signal changes we made to the PR check experience: a plain-English explanation of exactly why the suggested change eliminates the vulnerability. Not a link to documentation. Not a reference to the CVE. An explanation, derived from the code's specifics, of how the fix addresses the vulnerability. For the SQL injection example, the explanation would describe how replacing dynamic string interpolation with parameterized queries ensures that user input is treated as data rather than executable code and why that distinction closes the vulnerability. The combination of these two features, suggested fix and its explanation, mirrors how a senior security engineer would actually review code with a colleague: first making sure they understand the problem, then showing them what good looks like. Trust is built through reasoning. Every time Snyk explains its thinking, it gives developers the tools to develop their own security instincts, which is, ultimately, the most durable outcome. Great developer experience does not happen by accident These five principles got solidified by watching what broke, understanding why, and changing our approach. Great developer experience requires principles like these that can guide thousands of small decisions across product, engineering, and design. As we move into a future where AI and human developers collaborate more closely, these principles ensure that security remains a tailwind, not a hurdle. The emphasis is on recording not only the outcome, but the entire thought process that led to it. Large technology companies, which operate on a large scale and with high complexity, have refined this definition based on their practical experiences: Spotify defines an ADR as “a document that captures a decision, including the context of how the decision was made and the consequences of adopting it.” This definition highlights the importance of looking forward (consequences) and backward (context). Amazon Web Services (AWS) describes it as a document that “describes a choice that the team makes about a significant aspect of the software architecture they plan to build.” AWS emphasizes the significance of the decision, suggesting that not every choice warrants an ADR. Collectively, these definitions paint a clear picture: an ADR is a concise, justified record of an architecturally significant design choice that addresses a specific functional or non-functional requirement. Okay, but why use it? That is the central question. We use ADR to combat a silent but extremely costly problem in software projects: “ architectural amnesia.” You know when a new developer asks why we use technology X instead of Y, and the only answer is “it’s always been that way” or “the old team decided it”? Or when the team spends hours debating the same decision for the third time because no one remembers the original reasoning? That’s exactly where ADR comes in. It serves as your project’s memory, recording not only what was decided, but more importantly, why it was decided. In short, we use ADRs to ensure that critical knowledge about the architecture is not lost over time or when people leave, allowing the team to make future decisions faster, more confidently, and in a more informed manner. Okay, but what are the benefits of using ADRs? ADRs offer a return on investment that goes far beyond simple documentation, impacting the efficiency, culture, and sustainability of a project in the long term. For example, one of the most immediate and frequently cited benefits is the dramatic reduction in the time it takes to integrate new team members. Instead of relying on lengthy explanation sessions with senior engineers, a new developer can read the Architecture Decision Log (ADL) to gain a deep and contextualized understanding of the system. Another important example is cyclical debates. Engineering teams can spend a significant amount of time in cyclical debates on the same issues, a phenomenon known as “relitigation” of decisions. A well-written ADL puts an end to this. By documenting the context, the alternatives considered, the pros and cons of each, and the final rationale, the ADR establishes a baseline for the decision. Note: What is ADL? A single ADR is a snapshot of a decision. The real power of the practice, however, emerges from the collection of all ADRs for a project. This collection is known as the Architecture Decision Log (ADL). How do I know that this decision should become an ADR? This is a crucial question for keeping the process light and efficient. The rule of thumb is: a decision should become an ADR when it is “ architecturally significant.” To find out if this is the case, ask yourself a few key questions: Does the decision have an impact that goes beyond a single team or a single module? Is it difficult or costly to reverse in the future? Does it introduce new technology, a new standard, or a significant external dependency into the project? Does it resolve a technical issue that the team has debated at length and needs to be aligned on? If the answer to any of these questions is “yes,” then that decision is a strong candidate for becoming an ADR. For example, choosing a cloud provider (AWS vs. Google Cloud) deserves an ADR; however, the decision to refactor an internal function within a service probably does not. The goal is to record the choices that shape the long-term structure and constraints of your software. I understand, but how do I implement this in my workflow? Implementing ADRs in your workflow is an agile process that should live alongside your code. The most common and effective way is to treat architectural decisions as code. Start by creating a folder at the root of your repository, such as /docs/adr/. When a significant architectural decision needs to be made (e.g., adopting a new library, defining a communication standard between services), a team member creates a new Markdown file in that folder, using a simple template to describe the context, the decision, and the consequences. This file is then submitted as a Pull Request (PR). The decision is reviewed in the comments of this PR, involving the entire team. Once the proposal is approved, the PR is merged, and the ADR becomes an official and immutable record of the project. This way, you integrate documentation into the development flow that your team already knows, ensuring that it is collaborative, versioned, and always accessible. And how do I write an ADR? To write an Architecture Decision Record (ADR), you should focus on clearly and concisely documenting an architecturally significant decision. Start with a title that summarizes the decision and add a status (such as “Proposed,” “Accepted,” or “Replaced”) and the date. Next, describe the Context section, which should explain the problem, the forces at play, and the constraints that led to the need for a decision. This part is crucial so that future readers, including new team members, can understand the scenario at the time of the decision. The heart of the ADR lies in the following sections. In Options Considered, list the different approaches that were evaluated to solve the problem, briefly detailing the pros and cons of each. This demonstrates that the decision was not made lightly. Next, in the Decision section, clearly state which option was chosen and, crucially, justify “why.” The justification is the most important element, as it explains the logic and factors that led to the final choice. Finally, document the Consequences of the decision, both positive and negative. This helps the team understand the trade-offs and future impacts of the choice, such as new dependencies, changes in the way of working, or possible limitations. Keeping the ADR in a simple format, such as Markdown, and storing it alongside the project’s source code makes it easy for the entire team to access and consult. By documenting not only what was decided, but why and with what consequences, we transform volatile decisions into lasting knowledge for the team. Far from being a bureaucratic burden, the process of creating and maintaining ADRs, especially when integrated into the existing development flow, strengthens communication, accelerates the integration of new members, and ensures that your software evolves in a conscious and sustainable way. Adopting ADRs is, in essence, an investment in the clarity and longevity of your architecture. The model standardizes visuals, so everyone — from product managers to developers — aligns on what’s being built. Disconnect Between Diagrams and Code Diagrams that don’t match reality create friction. Developers draw “high­level fluffy conceptual pictures” that don’t reflect what the code actually implements. C4 demands that, at least by Level 3, the diagram maps to code structure. What is an Architecture Decision Record (ADR)? Before we move to Architecture Decision Record, first, we need to know what architectural decisions are. On Wikipedia, we can find that Architectural Decisions are, Architecture Decision are design decisions that address architecturally significant requirements; they are perceived as hard to make and/or costly to change. In his blog post, Documenting Architecture Decisions, Michael Nygard states that they maintain a significant collection of architectural records that can influence the structural and non-functional characteristics, dependencies, interfaces, and techniques used in system development. From these two explanations, we can understand that it is important to save the decisions that have been made when building the system, especially all architecturally significant decisions. If they use SQS, they automatically have to use the AWS Cloud provider. If they use RabbitMQ, they can use self-hosted or other vendors. Now the decision they choose will be stored in a record, it can be in the form of Google docs, Atlassian Confluence, Markdown on Github, etc. Now this record is called the Architecture Decision Record. With the growth of the team and the company, the people inside definitely come and go. Old people may resign, and new people will join. And this will bring an issue for new people when joining the company in the future. They may need to know, “Why was such a decision made”? Why did the previous team choose RabbitMQ over SQS? ADR components If we look at the picture above, there are several important components in ADR. Even if there are many versions of ADR on the internet, these five components are the most important components when making ADR. Title The first very important component is the Title. This will be the identifier of the ADR we are going to create. The format is free, but the most important part of this component is that it must have a number, followed by the main title of the ADR. Example: ADR 1: Using RabbitMQ over SQS or 1. Using RabbitMQ over SQS This number is useful as a reference and decision record versioning which I will explain later at the end of this article. Status This component represents the current state of the ADR that has been written. Usually, the stages start from the “proposed” when the ADR is still waiting for approval from all stakeholders involved. Then it is changed to “accepted” when all stakeholders agree. With business growth and the company becomes bigger, new regulations or new flow introduced, the decision may be no longer relevant, then the status will be changed to “deprecated” or “ superseded by  ” if there is a new ADR that replaces the obsolete ADR. Context This component explains the background of why  the ADR exists. Usually, this component is neutral and only explains the facts related to a problem. This component provides the existing options (example: option 1: using Golang, option 2: using JavaScript). Also would be better if we also put all the pro-cons between the options listed on this component. Decision This component describes decisions that have been agreed upon by all stakeholders involved. Here will be explained all the reasons why this decision was taken. Which will answer all the questions “Why….”  on the architectural design that has been made. Not “I will …” or “The CTO will …” because this is an individual decision, not a jointly agreed decision. Consequences This component will explain all the decisions' negative effects or drawbacks/tradeoffs. So that all stakeholders are aware that the decisions that have been agreed upon have tradeoffs that must be dealt with. The design was dead on arrival, not because it was technically unsound, but because it lacked architectural integrity. This is the reality of Enterprise Architecture Principles. They are not suggestions. They are the non-negotiable rules that override technical convenience. Architecture Principles represent the qualitative statements of intent that govern every architectural decision within an enterprise. Developed primarily through The Open Group Architecture Framework (TOGAF), these principles function as the primary mechanism through which the Architecture Review Board exercises governance. They establish an objective framework for evaluating project proposals, resolving conflicts between competing initiatives, and ensuring that tactical decisions remain aligned with strategic direction. The TOGAF Standard, Version 9.2, positions Architecture Principles as essential deliverables produced during the Preliminary Phase of the Architecture Development Method (ADM). Once stabilized and placed under change control, these principles become primary requirements that shape the entire enterprise architecture. They differ from policies or standards in their endurance: principles remain stable across technology cycles and organizational changes, while standards may evolve with specific technology versions. Organizations typically maintain between ten and twenty principles. This constraint prevents proliferation that would reduce flexibility and create governance overhead. The principles span four architectural domains: Business Architecture, Data Architecture, Application Architecture, and Technology Architecture. A fifth domain, Governance Principles, has emerged in practitioner communities to guide the architecture practice itself. Each principle follows a strict four-component template. The Name provides a memorable, essence-capturing identifier. The Statement articulates the fundamental rule in clear, unambiguous language. The Rationale explains why the principle exists, connecting it to business objectives and risk considerations. The Implications outline the consequences of principle adoption: what the organization must do, invest, or change to comply. This structure transforms abstract philosophy into actionable constraints. The contemporary enterprise operates in an environment of unprecedented technological complexity. Cloud migration, artificial intelligence adoption, cybersecurity imperatives, and distributed delivery create a landscape where technology decisions carry profound and often irreversible consequences. Without Architecture Principles, organizations face technology sprawl, integration failures, security vulnerabilities, and misaligned investments that fail to deliver business value. Principles provide the pre-positioned guidance that enables rapid decision-making without sacrificing architectural integrity. The absence of clear Architecture Principles manifests as ad-hoc decision-making. Similar situations receive different treatment based on who decides. Stakeholders cannot predict what architecture will accept. Architecture decisions require extensive negotiation because each decision is made de novo without established precedent. Technical debt accumulates despite architecture involvement. The organization develops inconsistent solutions that create integration headaches and higher costs. The root cause lies in starting the ADM without defined principles. Delivery pressure pushes teams to show immediate progress rather than invest time in foundational governance. Organizations underestimate the need for governance mechanisms, treating TOGAF as a documentation methodology rather than a governance framework. Political avoidance prevents principle development because it requires confronting conflicting interests across business units. The TOGAF standard provides twenty-one example principles that organizations can adopt or modify. These span the four domains, providing comprehensive coverage that practitioners leverage as starting points. The standard explicitly recommends limiting the total number to between ten and twenty, recognizing that excessive principles reduce architectural flexibility. Business Architecture Principles establish the connection between enterprise architecture and business strategy. They address strategic alignment, organizational effectiveness, and value creation. The example principles include: Maximize Benefit to the Enterprise, Business Continuity, Common Use Applications, Compliance with Law, and IT Responsibility. These prevent technology-driven decisions that neglect business context. Data Architecture Principles govern the management and use of information assets. They address concerns spanning technical implementation to organizational stewardship. The six standard principles are: Data is an Asset, Data is Shared, Data is Accessible, Data Trustee, Common Data Definitions, and Data Security. These elevate data from operational byproduct to strategic resource, justifying governance investment and quality attention. Application Architecture Principles guide software design, development, and deployment decisions. They address modularity, reusability, interoperability, and user experience. The four standard principles are: Technology Independence, Ease of Use, Buy Versus Build, and Interoperability. These shape the application portfolio that delivers business capabilities. Technology Architecture Principles direct infrastructure and platform decisions. They constrain technology diversity and ensure scalability, flexibility, and operational efficiency. The five standard principles are: Requirements-Based Change, Responsive Change Management, Control Technical Diversity, Interoperability, and Scalability and Flexibility. These mandate that infrastructure accommodate growth without fundamental redesign. Governance Principles, while not part of the original TOGAF 9.2 specification, have emerged as a critical fifth category recognized by practitioner communities. These include: Architecture Decisions Are Transparent, Architecture Compliance Is Verified, and Architecture Evolves Through Managed Change. They guide how architecture decisions are made, how exceptions are handled, and how the governance framework itself evolves. The standard template for defining principles contains four core components. The Name must be concise, distinctive, and technology-neutral. Examples like “Data is an Asset” or “Technology Independence” capture the essence while remaining memorable. The Statement must succinctly and unambiguously communicate the fundamental rule. It must be declarative, specific enough to guide decisions, and general enough to endure across technology cycles. The Rationale highlights the business value to the enterprise. It provides the foundation for justifying architecture activities and explains why the principle exists. Effective rationale statements quantify business value where possible, specifying metrics like “reduces integration costs by thirty percent through standard interface adoption” rather than unqualified assertions of efficiency. The Implications provide an outline of the key tasks, resources, and potential costs to the enterprise of following the principle. They provide valuable inputs to future transition initiatives and planning activities. Implications analysis must address four dimensions: technical (what architecture patterns or technologies are required), organizational (what roles or skills are needed), financial (what investments are required), and temporal (what is the implementation timeline). The mechanics of principle application occur throughout the ADM phases. In Phase A (Architecture Vision), principles establish the governance context for the architecture development. In Phase B (Business Architecture), Business Principles guide capability mapping and process design. In Phase C (Information Systems Architectures), Data and Application Principles constrain information and application portfolio decisions. In Phase D (Technology Architecture), Technology Principles guide platform selection and infrastructure design. In Phase E (Opportunities and Solutions) and Phase F (Migration Planning), principles guide consolidation and sequencing decisions. In Phase G (Implementation Governance), principles are enforced through compliance reviews. In Phase H (Architecture Change Management), principles are reviewed and updated as business strategy evolves. Implementing Architecture Principles requires a rigorous development process. Organizations must form a cross-functional Internal Architecture Board including senior management, business analysts, architects, and senior developers. This composition ensures diverse perspective integration, preventing technology-centric principles that neglect business context or business principles that ignore implementation reality. The development process draws from multiple source categories: business principles and strategic values, enterprise strategic goals, strategic drivers, pain points requiring solution, and best practices from other organizations. The Open Group explicitly acknowledges leveraging external principle sets where applicable, accelerating development through proven patterns. Stabilization and change control lock-down is essential. Principles that change frequently cannot guide consistent decision-making. The stabilization phase includes formal approval, communication, and training preparation. They are not replicated in individual project requirements specifications. Principle compliance verification occurs at every architecture review checkpoint: Architecture Vision, Business Architecture, Information Systems Architectures, Technology Architecture, and Implementation Governance. Exception handling requires explicit justification, risk assessment, and mitigation measures. The enforcement mechanism relies on the Architecture Review Board. Principles provide an objective framework for resolving conflicts between competing objectives: cost versus performance, time-to-market versus quality, standardization versus customization.
