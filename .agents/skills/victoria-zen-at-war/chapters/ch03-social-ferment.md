# Chapter 3: Early Buddhist Social Ferment

## Core Idea
To counter domestic and Western critics, late Meiji Buddhists developed "New Buddhism" (*Shin Bukkyo*), adopting Western science, secular education, and philanthropy. However, this apologetic push culminated in the near-unanimous endorsement and theological justification of Japan's expansionist wars against China (1894–95) and Russia (1904–05).

## Frameworks Introduced
- **New Buddhism (*Shin Bukkyo*)**: A late Meiji movement that sought to modernize Buddhist institutions, align doctrine with scientific rationality, engage in social welfare, and prove that Buddhism was the ultimate vehicle for patriotism and national unity.
- **Revere the Emperor and Worship the Buddha (*Sonno Hobutsu*)**: Promoted by the *Sonno Hobutsu Daidodan* (1889) to defend the Throne and exclude Christians from public office and political influence.
- **Peacetime vs. Emergency Religion**: D.T. Suzuki's 1896 framework. *Peacetime religion* involves productive social work (agriculture, science, arts) for humanity. *Emergency religion* commands soldiers to view their lives as light as goose feathers and sacrifice them on the battlefield to punish state enemies and preserve the nation.
- **Just War (*Bosen*)**: Shaku Unsho's Shingon framework distinguishing "just wars" (supported by Buddhism to punish lawless states and prevent human misery) from "lawless wars."

## Key Concepts
- **Imperial Rescript on Education (1890)** — The national moral code defining loyalty to the Emperor and filial piety as the ultimate virtues, which Buddhist reformers eagerly integrated into sectarian teachings.
- **Racial Familialism** — Inoue Enryo's concept that Asians (Japanese, Chinese, Koreans) are "golden-hued" brothers of the "Mongolian" race. This framed war with Russia as a compassionate Bodhisattva act to rescue Asian brothers from white, Christian imperialists.
- **Raja Dharma Supremacy** — The doctrinal stance (promoted by Shin sects) that the "Law of the Sovereign" is paramount, requiring absolute obedience as a religious debt of gratitude (*gokoku*).
- **Jiki (Directness)** — Rinzai master Nantenbo's koan teaching to General Nogi, defining Zen's core as moving forward without hesitation, mind-to-mind transmission, and *Yamato-damashii* (the spirit of Japan).

## Mental Models
- **Gate of Love (*Karuna*) and Gate of Knowledge (*Prajna*)** — Shaku Soen's synthesis: Knowledge operates in the absolute (transcending Samara/Nirvana, friend/foe, war/peace), allowing a calm soul amidst cannon fire. Love operates in the relative, striving to combat and subjugate evil to rescue fellow creatures.
- **The Bamboo Sheath Model** — Shaku Soen's explanation of self-sacrifice: Individual physical bodies are merely temporary sheaths of a bamboo sprout. For the spirit-plant (the nation) to grow, sheaths must be cast off without regret.

## Anti-patterns
- **Double-faced Apologetics** — Suzuki D.T. and Shaku Soen presenting Zen/Mahayana in the West as a science-compatible, peaceful, universal mysticism, while writing jingoistic tracts and serving as battlefield chaplains to inspire soldiers to kill.
- **Militaristic Nembutsu** — Encouraging soldiers to chant the name of Amida Buddha (*Nembutsu*) to secure Pure Land rebirth, transforming a practice of peace into a battlefield tool to eliminate the fear of death and increase killing efficiency.

## Worked Examples
### The 1893 World's Parliament of Religions
- **Apologetics as Warfare**: Shaku Soen and Suzuki D.T. traveled to Chicago to present Mahayana Buddhism as a rational world religion.
- **Wartime Rhetoric**: Delegate Yasubuchi Banryu viewed the Parliament as a "peaceful war" where Buddhism triumphed over Christianity, demonstrating that the international mission was conceived in competitive, nationalistic terms.

### The Port Arthur Conscription (1904)
- **Faith-based Soldier Selection**: Japanese military leaders intentionally recruited front-line troops from Kanazawa and Kumamoto (deeply Buddhist Shin-sect strongholds) to leverage their fearlessness of death.
- ** Nembutsu Charges**: Encouraged by sectarian tracts (like Osuka Shudo's), Ninth Division troops chanted Amida's name as they charged Russian lines, sustaining 15,605 casualties in a display of state-aligned religious zeal.

### Sawaki Kodo's Battlefield Zen (1904)
- Soto Zen master Sawaki Kodo (later famous for Antaiji) recounted his service in the Russo-Japanese War:
  - **"Gorging on killing"**: He bragged about picking off Russian soldiers in a hole, which his comrades praised as "just what you'd expect from a Zen priest... a man with guts." This shows how Zen training was tactically valued by peers for producing cold, efficient killers.

## Key Takeaways
1. "New Buddhism" was highly nationalistic, seeking to out-patriotize Christianity and secure state favor.
2. Rinzai and Soto Zen masters actively assisted in identifying the "essence of Zen" with Bushido and imperial conquest.
3. Russian/Western forces were labeled "enemies of the Buddha," transforming a political conflict into a holy war.
