# Chapter 12: Issues Awaiting Further Research

## Core Idea
The critical question facing future scholarship is whether "Imperial Way-Buddhism" and "Imperial State-Zen" were unprecedented aberrations or the logical culmination of long-standing historical and doctrinal antecedents. Zen and Buddhist scholars disagree, with some identifying deep systemic roots in Mahayana doctrine and pre-modern history, and others pointing to modern Meiji-era distortions, State Shinto infection, or an inherent ethical void within Zen itself.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Core Historical Question**: Determining whether Buddhist collaboration with state militarism is a modern heretical aberration (*distortion*) or a structural extension of pre-existing Buddhist ideas and historical state relationships (*adaptation*).
- **The Ethical Void Theory**: Winston King's framework suggesting that Zen cannot be "ethically distorted" because it possesses no intrinsic moral code or inner monitor; instead, it operates as a neutral "psychological technique for maximizing visceral energies" in any direction.
- **The Modern Construction Hypothesis**: Robert Sharf's framework stating that the "Zen" popular in the West is a modern creation of Meiji "New Buddhism" designed to serve nationalist ideology, and that the "Zen-Bushido unity" is a modern myth since the term *Bushido* is rarely attested in pre-Meiji literature.

## Key Concepts
- **Monastic Isolation from Social Suffering**: Abe Masao and Christopher Ives' critique that Zen's overwhelming emphasis on individual self-awakening (*satori*) led to a systemic failure to develop social ethics, resulting in default support for the status quo (aristocrats, samurai, militarists, or corporations).
- **Shinto Infection Theory**: Thomas Cleary's concept that Shinto "infected" Zen over the centuries, introducing authoritarianism, hierarchy, racism, ritual fetishism, worship of the dead/living, and physical body obsession, which Zen apologists then used to justify militarism.
- **The Martial Art Ruse**: Thomas Cleary's explanation that warriors and strategists used Zen as a ruse to dress alien, un-Buddhist elements of combat and machismo in the dignity of Buddhism, deceiving both Japanese and Westerners.
- **Kamakura Roots of Ultra-nationalism**: Nakamura Hajime's citation of Kamakura-era Zen monk Ean (1225–1277) to prove that Zen-backed ethnocentric nationalism and belief in Japanese cultural superiority predates the Meiji period by centuries.

## Mental Models
- **Zen As a Power Maximizer**: Regarding zazen and samadhi power (*joriki*) not as moral training, but as a psychological amplifier of raw focus and passion (e.g., the WWII lunge mine manual advising soldiers to attack "with spiritual vigour and steel-piercing passion").
- **The Status Quo Default**: The tendency of a religion without clear social ethics or transcendent criteria to align with whatever political power controls the state, whether feudal samurai, fascist militarists, or modern corporate interests.
- **Burdens of Historical continuity**: The recognition by Brian Victoria that scholars have a responsibility to trace the lineages of Buddhist-state relations back to Shakyamuni Buddha to maintain the integrity of historical research.

## Anti-patterns
- **Ethical Amnesia**: Promoting Zen in Western cultures as a pure, mystical, and peaceful practice while ignoring its historical function as a state-mobilized military training tool.
- **Scapegoating External Religions**: Attributing Zen's ethical failures entirely to external elements (like Shinto or Western imperialism) instead of engaging in critical self-examination of Buddhist texts and values.

## Worked Examples
### Dissenting Scholarly Perspectives
- **Abe Masao**: Acknowledged that Zen historically failed to clarify how to save others in social life, leaving no framework for human morality.
- **Christopher Ives**: Argued that monastic Zen’s lack of a critical spirit led to problematic support for samurai dictators, militarists, and corporations.
- **Robert Sharf**: Proved *Bushido* was a modern term exploited by propagandists, debunking the "ancient Zen-Bushido oneness."
- **Thomas Cleary**: Argued militarism distorted Zen and that Shinto "infected" it, but also claimed warriors used Zen to dress up un-Buddhist combat arts.
- **Winston King**: Concluded Zen has no scripture-based ethical monitor, serving merely as a psychological tool to maximize visceral energy (explaining Goro's combat mastery).

### Future Research Agenda
1. *Textual Analysis of the Founder*: Examining Shakyamuni Buddha's original teachings on state authority and war.
2. *Mahayana vs. Theravada*: Comparing Northern and Southern Buddhist schools regarding state cooperation and war doctrines to determine what is uniquely Mahayana or Zen.
3. *Pre-Modern State Relations*: Conducting comparative studies of Buddhist-state relations in India, China, Korea, and Japan over the centuries.

## Key Takeaways
1. Zen lacks a systematic, scripturally grounded social ethic, leaving it vulnerable to co-optation by the state.
2. The concept of Zen-Bushido unity is primarily a modern construction used to promote nationalistic agendas, though Tokugawa and Kamakura antecedents exist.
3. Resolving whether Zen militarism was an aberration requires analyzing the historical founder's position and comparing Mahayana developments with Southern Buddhism.
