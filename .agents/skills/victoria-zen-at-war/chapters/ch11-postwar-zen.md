# Chapter 11: The Post-War Japanese Responses to "Imperial State-Zen"

## Core Idea
The postwar critique of "Imperial State-Zen" was led almost single-handedly by Rinzai-affiliated scholar Ichikawa Hakugen, who dissected the historical and doctrinal pathologies of Japanese Buddhism. While individual leaders like Yamada Mumon and Asahina Sogen maintained nationalistic apologetics (justifying the war as a "holy war" that liberated Asia), the Soto Zen sect eventually issued a comprehensive "Statement of Apology" in 1992, catalyzed by exposures of discrimination in its overseas missionary history.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **The Twelve Pathological Characteristics of Japanese Buddhism**: Ichikawa Hakugen's framework identifying twelve historical, social, and doctrinal features that facilitated cooperation with fascism:
  1. *Subservience to the State (Gokoku Bukkyo)*: Indian Mahayana sutras used as state protection tools.
  2. *Karmic Justification of Inequality*: Karma doctrine misused to defend class divides and reject social reform.
  3. *Confucian-Style Obedience*: Shotoku's Constitution defining the Emperor as Heaven and subjects as earth.
  4. *Subversion of Muga (No-Self)*: "No-self" converted into the militarist slogan *Messhi Hoko* ("exterminate self, serve public").
  5. *Lack of Transcendent Dogma*: Focus on internal subjective feelings rather than external social ethics.
  6. *Convergence of On (Gratitude)*: Parental gratitude fused with imperial gratitude, eclipsing duty to "all sentient beings."
  7. *Organic Interdependence (State Family)*: Viewing the state as an organic family where the individual is subordinate.
  8. *Middle Way as Confrontation Avoidance*: Reducing the Middle Way to compromise, preventing structural reform.
  9. *Ancestor Veneration as Imperial Family*: Expanding ancestor worship to justify the expansionist slogan *Hakko Ichiu*.
  10. *Aestheticization of Resignation (Wabi/Sabi)*: Valuing obedience and acceptance as "spiritual maturity."
  11. *Anjin (Peace of Mind) over Justice*: Seeking private tranquility instead of building a just society.
  12. *Static Logic of Soku (Suchness)*: Embracing reality "just as it is," leaving no room for dynamic social critique.
- **Evangelical Pacification and Espionage adapter**: The model of overseas Buddhist missions serving as the imperial advance guard, using temple networks for cultural assimilation, pacification, and direct espionage for the military and secret police.

## Key Concepts
- **The Self-Loathing Remorse of Youth**: Yanagida Seizan's realization of his own "stupidity" and "self-loathing" postwar upon recognizing that his Zen devotion was a fanatical belief that "killing oneself on the state's behalf is the teaching of Zen."
- **Holy War Apologetics (Postwar Conservative Consensus)**: The belief maintained by Yamada Mumon and Asahina Sogen that Japan's war was a "holy war" because its "great sacrifice" resulted in the independence of Southeast Asian nations.
- **Double Level Mistake**: Otake Myogen's formulation of the Soto sect's errors: first, subordinating Buddhist teachings to worldly national policies, and second, taking away the dignity and identity of other peoples.

## Mental Models
- **Chameleon Flip-Around**: The rapid, shameless post-1945 transition of Buddhist leaders from warmongers to "peace advocates" overnight, without any systemic self-criticism.
- **Yasukuni Restoration Movement**: Postwar Rinzai leaders (Mumon and Sogen) actively lobbying to reinstate state funding for Yasukuni Shrine to venerate the "heroic spirits" of dead soldiers.
- **The Delayed Conversation**: Otake Myogen's model (citing E.H. Carr) that history is an endless conversation between past and present, which the Soto sect neglected for 48 years.

## Anti-patterns
- **Audience Split (Bilingual Duplicity)**: Presenting Zen as universalist peace to Westerners (e.g., Mumon's *A Flower in the Heart*), while extolling "holy war" and Yasukuni's "heroic spirits" to domestic audiences in Japanese.
- **Egolessness as Defensive Excuse**: Asahina Sogen attributing the loss of the war to Tojo and the military's "self-centeredness" (lack of *muga*), thereby avoiding the morality of the war itself.
- **Discriminatory History Cover-up**: Soto Zen publishing a history of overseas missions in 1980 that glorified colonial assimilation and used discriminatory language, forcing a recall and apology in 1993.

## Worked Examples
### Yanagida Seizan's Post-war Crisis
- **Eigenji Training**: Trained during the war convinced his devotion served the state.
- **Postwar Remorse**: Developed self-loathing, contemplated suicide, and criticized the "shameless" sectarian leaders who flipped smoothly to peace. Yet, he focused his research on early Chinese Ch'an rather than pursuing a systemic critique of Zen's war role.

### Yamada Mumon's Bilingual Apologetics
- **English version (1964)**: Described the war as a holy war only in a "feeble sense" as a stepping stone for Asian independence and expressed a desire to build atonement temples.
- **Japanese version (1976)**: Co-founded the *Association to Repay the Heroic Spirits*, declaring the war a "holy war" where Japan "destroyed itself" to give Asia independence, praising the "loyal, brave, and rival-less" spirits.

### Asahina Sogen's Military Critique
- Traced Zen state-service back to Mongol invasions.
- Blamed Tojo Hideki's jealousy of Admiral Yamamoto for Japan's defeat (inability to build planes), calling them "children fighting" and lacking Zen *muga*.
- Led training camps for wounded veterans, telling them to offer their remaining lives to the state.

### Soto Zen's 1992 Declaration of Repentance
- Administrative head Otake Myogen apologized in Soto Shuho (Jan 1993) for violating human rights in Asia, serving as imperial vanguard in Korea (depriving Koreans of names, language, identity), and conducting espionage.
- Restored priest Uchiyama Gudo's status in April 1993, 83 years after his execution.

## Key Takeaways
1. Ichikawa Hakugen single-handedly created the postwar study of Buddhist war responsibility, identifying deep doctrinal roots rather than simple moral failures.
2. Postwar Rinzai Zen leadership (Mumon, Sogen) actively supported neo-nationalist causes (Yasukuni funding, Emperor worship) and defended the war's outcome.
3. Soto Zen's 1992 apology is the most detailed institutional confession, recognizing the systemic abuse of Buddhism to strip colonial subjects of their identity.
