# Chapter 2: The Attempted Suppression of Buddhism

## Core Idea
The rise of the Meiji government in 1868 triggered a wave of state-backed anti-Buddhist persecution (*Haibutsu Kishaku*). To survive, Buddhist leaders aligned their institutions with the state's nationalistic, imperialist, and anti-Christian goals, laying the groundwork for complete subservience.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Separation Edicts (*Shinbutsu Hanzen-rei*)**: Decrees issued in 1868 that separated Shinto from Buddhism, purging Buddhist priests, statuary, and terms from Shinto shrine compounds. This was driven by the *National Learning* (*Kokugaku*) movement to cleanse Japan of foreign (Chinese/Indian) influences.
- **Imperial Law and Buddhist Law Unity (*Obo-Buppo Ichinyo*)**: The trans-sectarian principle formed by the *Alliance of United Sects for Ethical Standards* (1868) that pledged Buddhism's total alignment with state/imperial laws.
- **State Shinto (*Kokka Shinto*) vs. Sect Shinto (*Kyoha Shinto*)**: The 1882 division of Shinto. The government classified State Shinto's emperor-centric cultic rituals as "non-religious patriotic duties" to justify state funding, while labeling other sects (including Sect Shinto, Buddhism, and Christianity) as "religions" subject to private citizen rules.

## Key Concepts
- **Haibutsu Kishaku** — "Abolish Buddhism, Destroy Shakyamuni." The historical movement of iconoclasm and temple destruction during early Meiji.
- **Great Teaching (*Daikyo*)** — The state-devised national doctrine based on three principles: reverence for national deities/patriotism, Heavenly Reason/humanity, and unquestioning obedience to the Throne and authorities.
- **Doctrinal Instructors (*Kyodoshoku*)** — A state-sponsored, certified priesthood created in 1872 to propagate the *Great Teaching*. Over 81,000 Buddhist priests served in this system to escape persecution.
- **Order No. 133 (1872)** — The government directive permitting Buddhist priests to eat meat, marry, grow their hair long, and wear ordinary clothing. Initially requested by Soto Zen priest Otori Sesso to allow married priests to legally serve the state.
- **Separation of Religion and Rule (*Seikyo Bunri*)** — Shimaji Mokurai's campaign to separate political governance from sectarian religion, which eventually dismantled the joint Shinto-Buddhist Ministry of Doctrine in 1875.

## Mental Models
- **Tragedy of Religion (Bellah)** — "Every religion seeks to remake the world in its own image, but it is always to some extent remade in the image of the world."
- **Transactional Survival** — Leveraging resources (e.g., funding government debts, executing state propaganda) to secure institutional survival under a hostile political regime.

## Anti-patterns
- **Sacerdotal Sacrilege for Survival** — Replacing traditional Buddhist altars with Shinto deities and paying for these renovations (e.g., at Zojoji in Tokyo) in a desperate attempt to show loyalty to Shinto-dominated state authorities.
- **Constrained Freedom** — Accepting the constitutional "freedom of religious belief" (Article 28 of the 1889 Meiji Constitution) which was explicitly limited to "limits not prejudicial to peace and order, and not antagonistic to their duties as subjects."

## Worked Example
### The Satsuma Domain Persecution (1869)
The Satsuma domain (modern Kagoshima) serves as the extreme case study of early Meiji suppression:
- **Total Eradication**: All ~4,500 Buddhist temples and halls were closed or demolished.
- **Forced Conscription**: All priests aged 18 to 45 were forcibly returned to lay life and drafted directly into the newly formed Imperial Army, demonstrating how state-enforced secularization served the state's military build-up.
- **Re-education**: Priests over 45 were reassigned to state domainal schools, and those under 18 were returned to their families, effectively erasing the local Buddhist clerical lineage.

## Key Takeaways
1. The modern subservience of Japanese Buddhism was not a natural development, but a survival mechanism triggered by severe state persecution.
2. Order No. 133 effectively de-sacralized the Buddhist priesthood, turning monks into lay citizens with national/military obligations.
3. The constitutional guarantee of religious freedom was a legal facade, keeping religions subordinate to State Shinto and national duties.
