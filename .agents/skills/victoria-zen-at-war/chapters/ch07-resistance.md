# Chapter 7: Buddhist Resistance to Japanese Militarism

## Core Idea
Despite the near-unanimous war consensus of mainstream sectarian hierarchies, a courageous minority of young lay activists and individual priests resisted militarism, capitalism, and state fascism. Although their movements were systematically crushed by police suppression, their efforts programmatically demonstrated that resistance was indeed possible.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Youth League for Revitalizing Buddhism (*Shinko Bukkyo Seinen Domei*, 1931)**: A young, lay-dominated activist organization that rejected mainstream "bourgeois" sectarian Buddhism and called for a return to the historical Buddha's principles of love, equality, and freedom.
- **Return to the Buddha**: The League’s call to bypass corrupt, wealth-seeking sectarian hierarchies and apply the original, egalitarian spirit of the Dharma directly to modern socio-economic crises.
- **Buddhist Anti-nationalism**: Seno Giro's framework asserting that true peace requires international cooperation based on Buddhist "selflessness" (*muga*), rejecting narrow nationalism as a self-centered pathology that inevitably drives states to military force.

## Key Concepts
- **Sermon Thieves (*Sekkyo Dorobo*)** — Seno Giro's term for mainstream priests who claimed social and economic ills could be resolved through purely spiritual means, while living comfortably on wealthy donations and acting as ideological pawns for the ruling classes.
- **Opiate of the Masses** — The League's critique of mainstream Buddhism's claim to "transcend class differences and value harmony," arguing that in reality it functioned as an opiate to pacify and exploit the working class.
- **Fabrications and Wild Rumors (*Kyozo-setsu*)** — The criminal charge used by police to prosecute anti-war speech (such as Takenaka Shogan's) that contradicted imperial military propaganda.
- **Special High Police (*Tokko*)** — The secret political police who monitored religious figures, issuing instructions in 1937 to vigilantly control the "erroneous words" of Buddhist priests because of their high potential to influence the masses.

## Mental Models
- **Monastic Samgha as Socialist Model** — Just like Uchiyama Gudo, Seno Giro drew his blueprint for a post-capitalist society directly from the communal, property-less, and egalitarian lifestyle of the early Buddhist *samgha*.
- **Moral Preparation for Martyrdom** — Giro’s practice of using silent meditation to mentally prepare for the freezing cold of prison cells, transforming spiritual discipline into political resilience.

## Anti-patterns
- **Tenko (Forced Recantation)** — Under five months of relentless police interrogation, Seno Giro broke and signed a confession admitting to treason, promising to unconditionally support the Emperor. The police used this confession to arrest 200+ League members, destroying the movement.
- **Sectarian Ostracism of Resistors** — Mainstream Shin and Zen youth organizations actively policing and expelling anti-war members to protect their own standing with the government.

## Worked Examples
### The 1933 Expulsion of the Youth League
At the third national conference of the *All Japan Federation of Buddhist Youth* (held off-campus after host Otani University banned them):
- **Anti-Fascist Proposals**: League representatives (led by Giro) introduced resolutions opposing military ideology, and explicitly condemned Adolf Hitler and the Nazi Party's oppression of Jews, burning of books, and arrest of peace activists as "anti-Buddhist."
- **Institutional Backlash**: The Federation (controlled by both branches of the Shin sect) ignored these motions, passed a resolution thanking the Kingdom of Siam for recognizing Japan's puppet state *Manchukuo*, and expelled the League.

### Individual Resistors
- **Takenaka Shogan (1937)**: Higashi Honganji abbot of Myosenji who told departing soldiers and fellow priests that the war in China was "aggression," a "terrible loss," and that the state war budget was a "budget for murder." Charged with spreading rumors, his age (71) and religious reasoning saved him from prison, but he died under police surveillance in 1945.
- **Kondo Genko (1937–1941)**: Soto Zen abbot of Seiunji who preached that "killing of people is monstrous... there is nothing more sinful." Warned by police, he gave up his abbothood in 1941 and disappeared in Akita prefecture, never to be heard from again.

## Key Takeaways
1. The Youth League was the first explicitly religious organization in Japan to enter the path of active resistance against the fascist state.
2. Mainstream Buddhism's claim that religion "transcends politics" was selectively applied: it allowed support of state violence while banning progressive social reform.
3. The destruction of the League by 1942 eliminated all organized Buddhist opposition to the war effort.
