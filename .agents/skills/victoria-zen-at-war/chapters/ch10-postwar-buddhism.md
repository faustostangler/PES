# Chapter 10: The Post-War Japanese Responses to "Imperial Way-Buddhism"

## Core Idea
Following Japan's surrender on August 15, 1945, Imperial Way-Buddhism (*Kodo Bukkyo*) and Imperial State-Zen (*Kokoku-Zen*) formally collapsed. Post-war responses were slow, defensive, and divided: individual apologists like D.T. Suzuki minimized personal responsibility by blaming Shinto and Western influence, while institutional sects took over forty years to issue formal declarations of war responsibility.

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Shinto Blame Shift**: D.T. Suzuki's postwar framework attributing the conceptual origin of militarism, totalitarianism, and imperialistic expansion entirely to State Shinto, classifying it as a "primitive religion lacking spirituality" to shield Mahayana Buddhism and Zen from systemic culpability.
- **Double Truth / Absolute and Relative Truth (*Shintai and Zokutai*)**: The Shin (Pure Land) sect's doctrinal framework. In the postwar declaration of Nishi Honganji, this was identified as a concept that was "put to cunning use" to justify worldly cooperation with militarism under the guise of relative truth (*zokutai*) while keeping absolute truth (*shintai*) transcendent.
- **Collective Penitence of 100 Million (*Ichiogu So-zange*)**: The political and social framework of postwar Japan that diluted specific, individual, or sectarian war guilt into a generalized national confession, which Buddhist scholars argued led to the loss of Buddhist precepts and the evasion of religious war responsibility.

## Key Concepts
- **Ethnocentric Zen Superiority**: D.T. Suzuki's concept (asserted in his 1942 Japanese book *Toyoteki ichi*) that Westerners (and even most Orientals) are spiritually incapable of understanding Zen's deep insights, describing Occidentals as "deaf mutes" in the face of Zen spiritual life, reflecting underlying nationalist and xenophobic attitudes.
- **Zen Moral Indifference**: The positioning of Zen above all moral criteria (e.g., Suzuki's claim that Zen can be wedded to anarchism, fascism, communism, or democracy, and that whether an action is right or wrong can be looked at later).
- **The Double Lie**: Higashi Honganji's definition of calling the war a "sacred war" as a double lie—lying both to the public and to the Buddhist teachings of Shinran, while simultaneously converting participants into both victims and victimizers.

## Mental Models
- **Historical Avoidance (Easy Road)**: The tendency of institutional Buddhism to alter daily liturgies to reflect the end of militarism without engaging in critical self-examination, leaving the "predisposition" that affirmed war unexposed.
- **Zen Mystical Transcendence**: The construction of Zen by postwar apologists as an experiential, irrational, and ahistorical phenomenon, placing it completely above historical constraints and moral critique.
- **Victim/Victimizer Duality**: Koga Seiji's model stating that those who participate in war are simultaneously victims of the state and victimizers of other peoples, making war complicity a grave sin rather than a mere "mistake."

## Anti-patterns
- **Prewar-Postwar Chameleon**: Buddhist leaders who authored books praising the Emperor and battlefield killing during the war, only to immediately espouse "peace" after August 15, 1945, retaining high administrative and spiritual positions within their sects.
- **Studied Contempt / Audience Split**: Writing books in English presenting Zen as a peaceful, aesthetic, and universalist philosophy to Western audiences, while writing nationalistic, militaristic, and ethnocentric works in Japanese for domestic audiences.
- **Transgressions of Yasukuni Enshrining**: Traditional Buddhist sects endorsing the Shinto Yasukuni Shrine and declaring that the war dead served the Imperial Throne, comparing their military service to the "great work of a Bodhisattva."

## Worked Examples
### D.T. Suzuki's Post-war Dualism
- **Domestic Defensiveness**: In 1945, Suzuki blamed Shinto for Japan's militarism and claimed that if he had any responsibility, he shared it equally with all Japanese. In 1947 (*Nihon no reseika*), he called Shinto a "primitive religion" that should be "disposed of." He claimed he was "just not very interested in such things" as the Manchurian Incident, despite his continuous publications on Zen-Bushido battlefield utility.
- **Western Apologetics**: To his English audience (*Zen* edited by Abe Masao), he described the Pacific War as "ridiculous" and "completely without justification," yet "inevitable," highlighting the positive outcome of increased American interest in Zen.
- **Ethnocentric Assertions**: In 1942 (*Toyoteki ichi*), he wrote that no Occidental could understand Zen's spiritual life, calling them "deaf mutes" in the face of it.

### Critical Western and Scholarly Rebuttals
- **Arthur Koestler (1960)**: In *The Lotus and the Robot*, Koestler attacked the "mental acrobacy" of Zen that transformed a non-violent doctrine into the creed of the murderous samurai by short-circuiting reflection and discarding caution.
- **R.J. Zwi Werblowsky (1967)**: Criticized Suzuki's moral neutrality, pointing out that saying Zen can be wedded to fascism or communism means it could also be wedded to Nazism and its gas chambers.
- **John McRae**: Criticized Suzuki's experiential and ahistorical definition of Zen, noting the ultimate irony that Suzuki's emphasis on non-dogmatism became an inviolable dogma.
- **Robert Sharf**: Noted that Westerners systematically failed to recognize the nationalist ideology underlying modern constructions of Zen, suggesting Suzuki's lifelong effort to teach the West was bound to a "studied contempt for the West."

### Sectarian Declarations
- **Higashi Honganji (1987)**: First traditional sect to issue a war responsibility declaration, 42 years after the war. Administrative head Koga Seiji repented calling the war "sacred" and equating military service to the work of a Bodhisattva, calling it a "double lie" and expressing deep shame.
- **Nishi Honganji (1991)**: Repented of war cooperation and confessed that the sect's relative and absolute truth doctrine was "put to cunning use" to justify militarist collaboration.

## Key Takeaways
1. Institutional Japanese Buddhism took more than forty years (1987–1994) to begin issuing formal admissions of war responsibility.
2. D.T. Suzuki's postwar writings shifted blame to State Shinto and Western imperialism, while maintaining Zen's moral purity and his own innocence.
3. Postwar critics (both Western journalists/scholars and domestic lay advocates like Endo Makoto) identified a fundamental lack of moral awareness and historical contextualization in modern Zen.
