# Chapter 6: The Incorporation of Buddhism Into the Japanese War Machine

## Core Idea
Between 1913 and 1930, Japanese Buddhism was fully integrated into the state's domestic and colonial apparatus. Domestically, Zen scholars resurrected Bushido as a civic code of self-sacrifice. Internationally, Buddhist missions in Korea, Manchuria, and China actively collaborated with the military, serving as tools of cultural assimilation (*kominka*) and military espionage (*senbu*).

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Bushido as Civic Morality**: Nukariya Kaiten’s 1913 framework (*Religion of the Samurai*) defining Zen and Bushido not just for military elites, but as a mandatory code of bravery, manliness, and self-sacrifice for every citizen in the national "struggle for existence."
- **Evangelization Following the Military (*Senbu*)**: The missionary pattern where Buddhist sects advanced alongside invading Japanese armies, establishing temples to pacify occupied territories and spread "the benevolent influence of the Emperor."
- **Pacification Activities (*Senbu Kosaku*)**: A system of military espionage where continental mission priests acted as spies for military authorities, identifying anti-Japanese rebels under the guise of religious/charitable work.

## Key Concepts
- **Junshi (Following the Lord in Death)** — The ancient practice of committing ritual suicide (*seppuku*) upon a lord's death, exemplified by General Nogi Maresuke and his wife in 1912 following Emperor Meiji. Mainstream Zen apologists celebrated this act to inspire the rising generation to become "hundreds of Nogis."
- **Tenpai (Emperor Signs)** — Large tablets representing the Emperor placed directly on Buddhist altars next to Amida Buddha, systematically inculcating Emperor worship under Buddhist clothing.
- **Kominka Kyoiku (Imperialization Education)** — Assimilation programs run by continental Buddhist missions, including Japanese language schools, vocational training, and social welfare, to convert colonized peoples into obedient subjects.
- **Corporate Imperialism** — Financial complicity of Buddhist institutions (e.g., the aristocratic Otani family of Nishi Honganji holding major stock in the state-owned *South Manchuria Railway Company*).

## Mental Models
- **Pan-Asianist Imperialism** — The early Meiji sectarian vision (Ogurusu Kocho, Okumura Enshin) of using Buddhism to build an anti-Western alliance among Japan, China, and India. This directly anticipated the state's later "Greater East Asia Co-prosperity Sphere" ideology.
- **Renouncing the "Small Self"** — Shaku Soen's explanation of Zen self-sacrifice: abandoning the individual "small self" to serve the state's "greater cause," which he marketed to both Westerners and Japanese as the secret behind military victories.

## Anti-patterns
- **The Clerical Spy** — Utilizing the cover of priesthood and welfare to run espionage networks, culminating in the destruction of clerical identity documents to hide spying missions.
- **Co-opting Sacred Space** — Converting continental temples into temporary barracks and supply depots for invading troops.

## Worked Example
### Otani Sonyu's Prophetic Warning and Imperial Co-optation
The career of Shin sect leader Otani Sonyu demonstrates the absolute gravity of the state's co-opting force:
- **The 1921 Warning**: Sonyu published a prophetic essay warning that blind obedience to the state leads to spiritual slavery. He asserted that any state that ignores love and humanity, prioritizing its own material maintenance regardless of neighbor states, "is sure to meet a sad fate and lose its own existence."
- **The 1937 Fall**: Despite his warning, Sonyu abandoned his dissent. In 1937, he joined Konoye Fumimaro's cabinet as Minister for Colonial Affairs, overseeing the administration of Japan's occupied territories.
- **Economic Exploitation**: Sonyu served as President of the *North China Development Corporation*, directly managing the economic exploitation and resources extraction of conquered Chinese lands.

## Key Takeaways
1. Mainstream Zen successfully rebranded itself as the "Religion of the Samurai" to justify militarism to the youth.
2. Buddhist missions on the continent were not independent religious operations but administrative arms of the Japanese colonial empire.
3. Every single continental mission collapsed in 1945, proving they had no organic root among the local populations.
