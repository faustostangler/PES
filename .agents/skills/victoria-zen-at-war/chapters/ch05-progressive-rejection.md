# Chapter 5: Institutional Buddhism's Rejection of Progressive Social Action

## Core Idea
The High Treason Incident of 1910 sparked an intense backlash within the leadership of Japan's traditional Buddhist sects. Eager to distance themselves from the executed Uchiyama Gudo and imprisoned Takagi Kenmei, sectarian hierarchies issued abject apologies to the Emperor, condemned social reform as a heresy, and entered a formal, state-sponsored alliance to suppress "dangerous thoughts."

## Frameworks Introduced
- **Evil Equality (*Aku Byodo*)**: Rinzai Master Toyota Dokutan's theological term to condemn socialist and anarchist egalitarianism. Mainstream Zen asserted that "true equality" can only exist through social hierarchy (*sabetsu soku byodo*); attempting to eliminate social and economic classes was thus branded a heretical distortion of the Dharma.
- **The relative/ultimate bifurcation**: Higashi Honganji's framework separating relative truth (the secular realm where Raja Dharma/State Law is absolute and supreme) from ultimate truth (the transcendent realm of faith). This allowed the sect to demand absolute obedience to the state as a paramount religious duty.
- **Conference of the Three Religions (*Sankyo Kaido*, 1912)**: A landmark government-convened assembly of Shinto, Buddhist, and Christian leaders designed to unify all religious institutions behind the "Imperial Way" (*Kodo*), establish national morality, and cooperatively fight leftist radicalism.

## Key Concepts
- **Imperial Tablets** — The practice of placing a memorial tablet reading "May the current Emperor live for ten thousand years" directly in front of the central Buddha image in Rinzai temples, explicitly dedicating spiritual training to protecting the Throne.
- **Raja Dharma Supremacy** — Higashi and Nishi Honganji's doctrine that secular imperial law takes precedence over Buddhist law, making any anti-government action a betrayal of the sect.
- **Sectarian Vigilance** — Soto Zen directives instructing all subordinate temples and schools to perform strict self-censorship and monitor both priests and laity for "dangerous thoughts" to expiate Gudo's crime.

## Mental Models
- **Form vs. Formless Legitimation** — Utilizing the philosophical distinction between the temporal world of form (where differences in status, wealth, and power are permanent) and the transcendent world of formlessness to defend class structures and justify state-sanctioned violence.
- **State-Religion Co-dependency** — Viewing religion as the "spirit" and the state as the "body." Since their interests are unified, any service to the state is service to religion.

## Anti-patterns
- **Abject Apology to Power** — Soto Abbot Morita Goyu issuing a public apology on the eve of Gudo's execution, pleading that the sect is "profoundly dumbstruck" by Gudo and declaring that Soto Zen's core mission is "respecting the Emperor and protecting the state."
- **Nailing the Coffin of Independence** — Voluntarily participating in the 1912 *Sankyo Kaido*, which effectively destroyed any remaining institutional independence, turning temples and churches into permanent ideological instruments of the state.

## Worked Examples
### The Sonno Aikoku Ron (Essays on Reverence for the Emperor and Patriotism, 1912)
Published to clear "the greatest disgrace of the Meiji period," this compilation by 15 leading thinkers sought to "exterminate vermin and fill up ant holes" of radicalism:
- **Ouchi Seiran's Anti-Christian Polemic**: Seiran argued that Christianity must be banned because its absolute monotheism makes it impossible to revere the Emperor, and it rejects Buddhist causality.
- **Inoue Enryo's Sacred Family Syllogism**: Enryo formulated that:
  1. The Japanese land is sacred.
  2. The Imperial House is sacred.
  3. Therefore, all subjects (as children of the Emperor/gods) are sacred.
  4. Consequently, Japanese loyalty is sacred, unlike the "private and lifeless" patriotism of the West, which is based on selfish individualism.

## Key Takeaways
1. Institutional Buddhism used the doctrine of karma and Zen philosophy to actively oppose socio-economic equality.
2. The government's inclusion of Christians in the 1912 conference proved that war-time patriotism was the ultimate currency for state legitimacy.
3. Post-1912, no major religious leader in Japan would organize opposition to the state's domestic repression or foreign wars.
